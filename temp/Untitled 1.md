## Part C: Summary (4-6 sentences)

The brain can be understood as having layers of evolutionary development, from the oldest "ancestral" structures to the newest, most complex regions. The "old brain," including the brain stem, medulla, pons, and cerebellum, controls basic survival functions like heartbeat, breathing, and movement. The limbic system, containing the amygdala, hypothalamus, and hippocampus, handles higher-order functions like emotion, memory, and regulation of body processes. The cerebral cortex, our "grey matter," is divided into four lobes (frontal, parietal, occipital, and temporal) that control our most advanced abilities including thinking, speaking, and perceiving. The myth that we only use 10% of our brains is false - brain scans show nearly all regions are active even during simple tasks. Phineas Gage's famous case demonstrated how physical damage to specific brain regions can dramatically alter personality and behaviour.

    - a) Perception
    - b) Sensory detection
    - **c) Sensation** ✓
    - d) Neurocognition
3. The retinal receptors are called **rods** and **cones**
    
4. **True or False: The optic nerve is what carries the neural impulses from the eyeball to the brain.** **TRUE** - The axons of ganglion cells braid together to form the optic nerve, which carries neural impulses from the eye to the brain.
    

## Part C: Summary (4-6 sentences)

Sensation and perception are two different but related processes in how we experience the world. Sensation is the bottom-up process where our senses receive and relay outside stimuli, while perception is the top-down way our brains organize and interpret that information. The absolute threshold is the minimum stimulation needed to detect a stimulus 50% of the time, and this detection can be influenced by psychological factors like alertness and expectations (Signal Detection Theory). Our eyes process light through the cornea, pupil, and lens, which focuses images onto the retina containing rods (for grayscale and peripheral vision) and cones (for color and fine detail). The retinal receptors convert light energy into neural impulses that travel via the optic nerve to the visual cortex in the brain. We perceive differences on a logarithmic rather than linear scale (Weber's Law), meaning we notice percentage changes rather than absolute amounts of change.

## Part C: Summary (4-6 sentences)

Our senses of hearing, taste, smell, and touch work through complex biological processes to help us interact with our environment. Sound waves vibrate through the ear canal, causing the eardrum and three ossicle bones (hammer, anvil, stirrup) to vibrate, which moves fluid in the cochlea and triggers hair cells that send signals to the brain. We taste five distinct flavors (sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami) through taste buds, and our sense of smell is intimately connected to taste through sensory interaction. Touch combines four distinct sensations - pressure, warmth, cold, and pain - and works with kinesthesis (body movement and positioning) and the vestibular sense (balance and head position) to help us navigate our world. Synesthesia is a rare condition where two or more senses become intertwined, possibly due to crossed neural connections, retained infant sensory mixing, or unusual neurochemistry. The emotional power of smell comes from its direct connection to the limbic system, linking scents closely with memories and emotions.
