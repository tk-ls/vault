**1. Why did Linus Pauling win the Nobel Prize in 1951?**

Linus Pauling won the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1951 for characterizing the alpha helix structure of common proteins. He used X-ray crystallography to show how common proteins fold up into elegant little spirals. This was a major breakthrough in understanding protein structure and set the stage for later work on DNA structure.

**2. Where do all life on earth came from, according to the Miller-Urey experiment?**

According to the Miller-Urey experiment (1953), all life on earth arose in a primordial soup of basic nutrients billions of years ago. Stanley Miller and Harold Urey produced amino acids—the building blocks of life—out of an electrified broth of non-living nutrients, supporting the idea that life originated from simple chemical reactions in Earth's early atmosphere and oceans.

**3. Define Meprobamate**

Meprobamate (marketed as Miltown) was the first of the "minor tranquillisers"—what we now call anti-anxiety medications. It became the bestselling drug in the U.S. by the late 1950s, with an estimated five percent of Americans taking it. Meprobamate was the first scientifically created, advertising-promoted drug for the "worried well"—people who mostly functioned in society but experienced anxiety.

**4. How did George Gey obtain the HeLa cells?**

George Gey, a cell biologist at Johns Hopkins, obtained HeLa cells from Henrietta Lacks, an African-American woman who came to the hospital in 1951 with cervical cancer. He took a biopsy (tissue sample) from her tumour and kept it alive in a machine called a roller drum. Lacks never consented to having her cells used for research, and her family wasn't aware of HeLa's existence until 1975.

**5. Define Sanger sequencing.**

Sanger sequencing is a method of decoding DNA developed by Frederick Sanger in 1977. It works by chopping up an unknown DNA sequence, tagging the segments with four different fluorescent dyes that bond to the four different nucleic bases, and then sorting out the segments by length. The process can be repeated many times to decode an entire genome. It became the standard DNA sequencing method until the late 1990s.

**6. What is the goal of the Human Genome Diversity Project (HGDP)?**

The HGDP was organised at Stanford in the 1990s with the mission to collect DNA samples from thousands of different populations worldwide to understand human diversity. Its founder, Luca Cavalli-Sforza, believed it would fight racism and celebrate different cultures by showing genetic similarities across populations. However, critics accused it of exploiting indigenous people for potential commercial gain, calling it "the Vampire Project."

---
**7. Why was the ARPANET created?**

ARPANET was created by the Defence Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) to develop advanced military technology and communications networks. It used packet switching technology—sending data in small, independent, broken-up parts that could each find their own fastest routes and be reassembled later. ARPANET became the precursor to the modern Internet.

---
**8. What is the purpose of the World Wide Web?**

The World Wide Web was invented by Tim Berners-Lee in 1990 to create the beginnings of the functional and useful web we know today. He built the first web browser to make the Internet accessible and user-friendly, allowing people to navigate between linked documents and information. The Web transformed the Internet from a technical network into a platform for sharing information globally.
