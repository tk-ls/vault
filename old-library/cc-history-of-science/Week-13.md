## 37. Air Travel and the Space Race
1. **Orville** and **Wilbur Wright** wanted to build a **flying machine**. They made lots of gliders and eventually a powered plane. They used **wood** and **fabric**, with a petrol-powered internal **combustion engine**, and some bicycle parts. ✓
2. Aviation took off for **war**, but also for **mail** and **passenger services**. ✓
3. With a more advanced engine, **Charles Lindbergh** flew across the **Atlantic** in 1927. ✓
4. **Science fiction** matters. It influences how we, including real-life **scientists and engineers**, think about what the **future** can be. In this case, **Jules Verne** was notable for trying to imagine a pretty dang realistic plan for **space exploration**, given 19th-century technology. ✓
5. Like aeroplanes, **rockets** changed warfare forever. ✓
6. Why did the Smithsonian curator argue that rockets on display should be pointed down? **So that visitors would be confronted with destruction—rather than pointed up and away, which implies victory without consequences.** ✓
7. The **USSR** launched the first satellite, **Sputnik**, on October 4, 1957. Only a few years later, in 1961, the Soviet Union sent the first **human into space (Yuri Gagarin)**. ✓
8. The **Apollo** program successfully landed humans on the **moon**. ✓
9. On July 20, the **Eagle Lander** touched down in the moon's **Sea of Tranquility**. **Neil Armstrong** became the first human to set foot on a planetary body other than the Earth. He was joined by **Buzz Aldrin**. ✓
10. The **Apollo** program was a great example of **big science**—research projects so big that no individual lab can do everything from beginning to end, so work is broken off into chunks. ✓
11. Space science raises **tough questions** about power and knowledge, shared resources and **competitions** between nations. ✓
## 38. Ecology
1. **Ecology** became a formal science in the late **1800s** and early **1900s**. ✓
2. Early ecologists tended to include both **life scientists** **like Haeckel and earth scientists**; as well as those studying evolution, like **geneticists** working on flies, and those studying landscapes, like **botanists**. ✓
3. How does **geochemistry** differ from **bio-geochemistry**? **Geochemistry uses the methods of chemistry to understand minerals, while bio-geochemistry analyses living and nonliving processes.** ✓
4. **Frederic Clements** argued that plant formations are best studied as units called **communities**. ✓
5. Both **Arthur Tansley** and Clements agreed that ecologists should promote **conservation**, or working to actively maintain the health of nonhuman environments. ✓
6. **George Evelyn Hutchinson**, **Howard T. Odom**, and **Eugene Odom** established ecosystem ecology as a richly **quantitative** discipline. Hutchinson was the first to use very small amounts of **radioactive particles** as **tracers** to map how particles move around in a pond, including how **plants** take up **radiation**. ✓
7. What is **systems ecology**? **The holistic study of complex living systems as systems, including the interactions among their nonliving inputs, their boundaries, how they adapt to new conditions, how they interact with other systems, and the unpredictable, emergent properties they exhibit.** ✓
8. The question of life in **space** intrigued scientists modelling the relationships between **energy**, **chemicals**, and **organisms**. ✓
9. What is the **Gaia Hypothesis**? **The idea that the earth's bio-geosphere is self-regulating, within broad limits: living things, air, rocks, and water interact in complex ways so that living things can stick around.** ✓
10. **Earth Systems Science** means understanding how living and nonliving processes **relate**, and looking at the entire earth as one very big—but not infinite— **house**. ✓
## 39. Controlling the Environment
1. Plants need the nutrient **nitrogen** to grow. But plants can't "fix" their own nitrogen from air, so they need **bacteria**, or human-made **fertilisers** like ammonia, to do it for them. ✓
2. What were the environmental problems with the **Haber-Bosch process**? **The process requires fossil fuels like oil or natural gas to work, and burning these fuels disrupts the earth's climate cycles. Also, runoff occurs when rain washes leftover fertilizer into waterways, leading to eutrophication (too many nutrients in the water), which causes algae build-up that uses up oxygen and makes water deadly for fish.** ✓
3. In the 1930s, after decades of research, agricultural scientists in the U.S. rolled out **hybrid crop varieties**. Commercial fields became **mono-culture**, or "one-plant". ✓
4. This is the opposite of growing many plants together, or **poly-culture**, which can help restore nutrients to soil without synthetic chemicals. ✓
5. The **Dust Bowl** forced many farmers to abandon their farms during the **Great Depression**. ✓
6. What was the **Green Revolution**? **A revolution in the 1950s and 60s when crop yields went up significantly for farmers in less industrialized countries through the use of hybrid seeds, synthetic nitrogen, and pesticides.** ✓
7. In the longer-term, the Green Revolution meant that more farmers started practising mono-culture, essentially betting their chips on a **small number of hybrid crops**. **Thousands of traditional varieties are no longer cultivated.** ✓
8. Only four crops—**wheat**, **maize**, **rice**, and **soy**—provide more than half of our food today. ✓
9. American biologist and pioneering environmentalist **Rachel Carson** wrote a series of articles that became the book **Silent Spring** in 1962. Carson helped spark the modern **environmentalism** movement in the United States. ✓
10. In 2006, Nobel-winning atmospheric scientist **Paul Crutzen** called for humanity to use **geoengineering**, the modification of our climate, to keep our world habitable. ✓
