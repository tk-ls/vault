## 7. Medieval Islamicate World
1. Who are Islamicate scholars? Scholars meaning people influenced by Islamic civilisation, regardless of their religious views. ✓
2. Islamicate power expanded rapidly after the Prophet Muhammad's death in CE 632. ✓
3. The blend of cultures and belief systems made early Islamicate science cosmopolitan—that is, generally inclusive in character. Further, Islam—the religion called on its adherents to treat others as equals, helping make Islamicate knowledge production more egalitarian. ✓
4. Caliph al-Ma'mun, in 832, refounded the House of Wisdom as an international centre for translation and research. By 850, the House of Wisdom had become the largest library in the world. ✓
5. What was the Translation Movement? How did it begin? It was a movement to bring useful texts into Arabic from Greek, Chinese, Sanskrit, Persian, and Syriac. It began with Persian texts concerning astrology and astronomy, sponsored by the caliphs. ✓
6. Many translators of Baghdad particularly fell for the works of Aristotle. ✓
7. The Translation Movement also fostered a strong appreciation for reasoned thought, at least among the ruling and scholarly classes. ✓
8. In mathematics, medieval Islamicate scholars focused on arithmetic and algebra. They also developed trigonometry. ✓
9. The Abbasid State privileged public service and the interests of the state, focusing on improving useful arts such as hydraulic engineering and agricultural science.10. What was the Book of Ingenious Devices? What's one example from the book? A compendium of one hundred devices written by the Banū Mūsā brothers in 850. One example is "The Instrument that Plays by Itself," the earliest programmable machine. ✓
10. Regarding the history of medieval Eurasian and North Africa, we encounter urban centres of trade and knowledge exchange populated by natural philosophers with a keen desire to build upon earlier insights regardless of their origins. ✓
## 8. Medieval China
1. Like Egypt, Sumer, and Mesoamerica, ancient China represents a hydraulic civilisation—one that maintained its population by diverting rivers to aid in irrigation—and one that developed writing thousands of years ago. ✓
2. From the beginning, science in China was a product of the state. ✓
3. What was the Chinese culture's grand model of the cosmos? In an infinite, empty space enclosed by the great celestial sphere, celestial bodies float around, directed by a "hard wind." The earth sits still at the centre, with the Middle Kingdom (China) at the centre of earth, and the Son of Heaven (emperor) at the symbolic centre of China. ✓
4. The most prominent early Chinese thinker was Confucius, whose philosophy emphasised the importance of tradition, etiquette, respect for elders, and for the patriarchy. ✓
5. What was the purpose of state examinations during the Han Dynasty? To ensure that the central administration had enough trained civil servants to oversee the collection of taxes and building of roads, maintain a large standing army, and roll out agricultural reforms. It also gave examinees a chance to jump from a lower class to a higher one. ✓
6. In 1012, the Song state introduced new early-ripening and winter-ripening rice from the Champa Kingdom in what is now Vietnam that allowed rice to be produced faster with less water.7. The mandarinate provided social stability and some insulation against corruption. ✓
7. What were some of the Chinese "firsts"/technical inventions? The wheelbarrow, silk production, earthquake monitors, lacquer, gunpowder, the crossbow, porcelain, umbrellas, fishing reels, suspension bridges, and paper money. ✓
8. Alchemy—or a systematic investigation of "what is stuff"—also took off with state support, starting in the Han Dynasty. ✓
9. The real achievements of Chinese technology were in infrastructure. ✓
10. The Grand Canal took infrastructure to a new scale. ✓
11. Chinese/Useful science was prioritised, not science for its own sake. ✓
## 9. Ancient and Medieval Medicine
1. Early medical systems allowed people to make sense of bodies and health. ✓
2. In Song Dynasty China's traditional Chinese medicine, health means a balance between two forces, yin and yang, representing dark and light, femininity and masculinity, hot and cold, and so on. ✓
3. Disease means imbalance. Thus healthcare means restoring balance, in TCM, by manipulating the energy/qi that flows through living bodies, called qi. ✓
4. If you lived in Gupta Dynasty India, you would study the science of life, Ayurveda. In addition to anatomy, the samhitas would also teach you etiology, or what causes different diseases, and symptomatology, or what diseases look like. ✓

6. His oath—"do no harm"—still underpins medical education. ✓
7. Galen built on the systems of Hippocrates and Aristotle, but also made detailed notes on human anatomy drawn from experience. ✓
8. What were some accomplishments of Persian polymath Abū Bakr al-Rāzī? He wrote dozens of books including detailed accounts of his cases, is considered a founder of several disciplines from psychology to ophthalmology, was the first to describe smallpox and measles as distinct diseases, and wrote for general audiences about health and disease. ✓
