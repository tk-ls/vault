## 31. Marie Curie and Spooky Rays
1. **X-rays** are named after the letter mathematicians use to **symbolise unknown variables**. ✓
2. **Radiology** is using **radiation** to see the inside of a body. ✓
3. What is **radiation**? **Energy that comes out of matter as it decays, or breaks down into other, smaller forms of matter.** ✓
4. **Marie Curie** would later name this process of decay "**radioactivity**". ✓
5. **Antoine Becquerel** also researched what happens when you mix radiation and **magnetic fields**, showing that radiation can have an **electrically** negative, positive, or neutral **charge**. ✓
6. What did you find most interesting about Marie Curie's backstory/background? Explain. I found it most interesting that Marie attended a secret school called the Flying University in Poland because Russians had outlawed lab science and the University of Warsaw didn't admit women. Despite not speaking French well and having no formal science training, she earned degrees with distinction in both physics and mathematics at the University of Paris. She was also denied a professorship at Jagiellonian University simply because she was a woman, despite being brilliant. She conducted groundbreaking research in an unventilated shed while pregnant, showing incredible dedication to science despite facing discrimination and difficult conditions. ✓
7. Marie used a sensitive **electrometer**, which measured electric charge and was developed years earlier, to test how ray-producing **uranium** affected **electromagnetic** fields. ✓
8. Marie created, for the first time, a theory of **radioactivity**: in some substances, atoms themselves must be **breaking down** slowly, **releasing energy**. This theory became **foundational** for **modern physics**. ✓
9. What other accomplishment of Marie Curie did you find most interesting? Explain why. I found it most interesting that Marie set up mobile radiography units (X-ray systems) during World War I to help field doctors treat soldiers. This showed her dedication to using science for practical, humanitarian purposes even during wartime. She literally brought X-ray technology to the battlefield to save lives. Additionally, her work using radioactive materials to treat cancer was groundbreaking and showed how she applied theoretical discoveries to help people, even though this work ultimately contributed to her death from cancer in 1934. ✓
10. Marie applied her theory. She used radioactive materials to treat **cancer**. ✓
11. What is the **Cori cycle**? **The cycle of how glycogen (a form of sugar) breaks down in muscles into lactic acid, and then is reformed as a source of energy. It was discovered by Czech-American biochemist Gerty Cori, who won the Nobel Prize for this discovery in 1947.** ✓
## 32. Einstein's Project
1. By 1900, **physics** was perceived by many to be an almost **completed discipline**. ✓
2. What is the photoelectric effect? The paradox that certain metals produce electrical currents when zapped with wavelengths of light above a certain threshold. It was discovered by Heinrich Hertz and helped show that light behaves as particles (quantum units) rather than just as waves. ✓
3. In trying to explain the weird results about light and heat, German physicist **Max Planck** theorised that light may not be a wave after all, but a series of **particles** or **quantum units**. ✓
4. What did you find most interesting about Albert Einstein's background/upbringing? Explain. I found it most interesting that Einstein was not an academic hotshot but a working-class nobody who did physics on the side. He dropped out of high school, couldn't get a teaching job because he was Jewish, and ended up working at a patent office. Despite being an outsider with no prestigious academic position, he revolutionised physics during his "miracle year" of 1905 at age 26. His wife, Serbian mathematician Mileva Marić, was also super smart and supportive. It shows that groundbreaking science can come from unexpected places and that formal credentials aren't everything. ✓
5. What four papers published by Einstein helped change physics overnight? 1) Brownian motion (the random motion of particles in fluids), 2) the photoelectric effect (supporting the idea of energy as particles, not waves), 3) the equivalence of mass and energy (E=mc²), and 4) special relativity. ✓
6. Einstein got rid of all reference frames for **space** and **time**. All measurements became relative to the **position** and **speed** of the observer. ✓
7. The only constant in the entire system became the **speed of light**. ✓
8. How does special relativity differ from general relativity? Special relativity is about comparing physical effects from different observer positions in terms of velocity (speed in a particular direction). It proved nothing can move faster than light and that space and time became one mathematically continuous spacetime. General relativity, published in 1915, provided all the complicated maths regarding relativity and acceleration (speeding up or slowing down). General relativity explains the physics of all situations, while special relativity is one specific case of general relativity. General relativity also showed that gravity is not a force like light, but an effect—a distortion in the shape of space due to mass. The planets follow certain paths not because of the sun's gravitational attraction, but because the space before them is curved by the sun's mass. ✓
9. Einstein's universe wasn't a series of perfect spheres in an ether, but a **void** whose very dimensions—whose rules, basically, other than the speed of light—could **change**. ✓
10. Einstein also contributed to the birth of **modern particle physics / quantum mechanics**. ✓
11. Einstein always **regretted** that his work was used for **violent ends / warfare / atomic weapons**. ✓
## 33. The Atomic Bomb
1. The U.S. government, with a little help from Canada, ran an enormously expensive and secret **weapons program** for four years. This program was code-named the **Manhattan Project**. ✓
2. In December of 1942, **Enrico Fermi**, the "architect of the nuclear age", created the very first controlled **nuclear chain reaction**—**Chicago Pile 1**. ✓
3. What was happening in the mines on the Navajo Nation starting in 1944? The Diné people (who call themselves the Diné) mined a yellow rock containing naturally high amounts of the heavy metal uranium. The Diné continued to mine the nation's uranium for decades until 1989, long after the peak of the Cold War. The mining of radioactive ore led to higher rates of lung cancer due to radiation exposure. The U.S. government didn't act to address this problem until 1990, a long time after World War II. ✓
4. American physics boss **Julius Robert Oppenheimer** oversaw the project's scientific **research** and the **design** of the nuclear weapons. ✓
5. On July 16, 1945, the team led by Oppenheimer set off the world's first **atomic explosion / atomic bomb test** at the Trinity site near Los Alamos. ✓
6. What two things did Oppenheimer recognise? 1) Developing a nuclear weapon was like being a god (he quoted "I am become Death, destroyer of worlds" from the Bhagavad Gita), and 2) This weapon was about appearing more fearsome to one's enemies. ✓
7. What two reasons factored into the decision to drop the atomic bomb? 1) To justify its monumental cost (the Manhattan Project was enormously expensive), and 2) To intimidate all enemies—present and future—of the United States. (Note: The reason cited at the time—to prevent a long, drawn-out war with Japan—is considered wrong by most historians today, as Japan was already militarily weak and an American victory was inevitable.) ✓
8. About **two hundred thousand** people died in the two bombings and in the months afterward. Japan **surrendered** unconditionally. It remains the **only nation** to have been attacked using an atomic weapon. ✓
9. During the **Cold War**, physicists came up with the thermonuclear or **hydrogen bomb**, which used **fusion** or the joining together of nuclei to create an even bigger reaction than the first-generation, fission reactors had been able to. ✓
10. **Nuclear waste** is incredibly dangerous for thousands of years. ✓
