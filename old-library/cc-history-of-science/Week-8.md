## 22. Darwin and Natural Selection
1. The belief that the living world was created by a kindly but hands-off God. ✓
2. **Charles Darwin** read the **geological theories** of **Charles Lyell** and think about **gradual change** over long ages. ✓
3. The main thing the voyage did was give Chuck access to hundreds of **specimens** from similar looking species that lived close to each other, but in slightly different **environments**. ✓
4. The most famous examples come from the **Galapagos Islands** off the coast of **Ecuador**. ✓
5. The different species seemed to **vary** according to island. This turned out to be one in a number of clues that would lead Check to developing the theory of **evolution** by **natural selection**. ✓
6. Darwin reasoned that living beings **compete** over resources, and only the most fit for a given region **survive**. ✓
7. The book explained "descent with modification" or "transform-ism" - showing natural variation in traits, environmental changes favouring some traits, with the fittest surviving and reproducing. ✓
8. **Alfred Russel Wallace** met Henry Walter Bates in 1844. They became science besties and decided to figure out how evolution works in order to apply that knowledge to human society and **save people** from greed and individualism. ✓
9. Wallace invented the discipline of **bio-geography**, or the study of **plants**, **animals**, and **geological formations** together. ✓
10. The reaction to natural selection was mostly **acceptance**. ✓
11. Darwinism quickly became equated with the term "**survival of the fittest**." ✓
## 23. Eugenics and Francis Galton
1. **Francis Galton** was the father of **eugenics**, the idea that the **gene pool** of the human species could somehow be **improved**, if certain people with different abilities **didn't have kids**. ✓
2. Social Darwinism is the application of "survival of the fittest" to human society, arguing that class conflict was a test of fitness. ✓
3. For Galton, as for most Victorians, **nature** held all the cards. He argued that an organism's most important **characteristics** must be **biological**, rather than shaped by environment or experience. ✓
4. His book, "Hereditary Genius", popularised the practice of **historiometry**, or studying **human traits** by tracking **ancestry** information. ✓
5. He proposed studying **twins**, which he saw as a natural experiment. By the mid-1900s, twin studies became the foundation of **behavioural genetics**, or how heredity affects behaviour. ✓
6. If nature is more powerful than nurture, twins should be similar even when raised apart; if nurture is more powerful, twins raised apart should behave differently. ✓
7. Galton's work with **peas** led him to conclude that traits tend toward a **statistical average**. He used statistics to model the general pattern of how traits were distributed over time—in this case, in a "**normal distribution**", or bell curve. ✓
8. Driven by a **fear** that births of supposedly inferior people would lead to work or criminally "degenerate" adults, some states introduced forcible **sterilisation** laws starting in 1907. ✓
9. (Open-ended answer - could discuss forced sterilisation, Nazi adoption of eugenics, or discrimination against people with disabilities) ✓
10. By the late 1920s, eugenics had been recognised as **bad science** by most biologists. ✓
## 24. Micro-biology
1. Single-celled organisms are also known as **microbes** or germs. ✓
2. The **cell** is the fundamental unit of life, the thing that gives **structure** to all forms of life. ✓
3. Huxley proposed that cells are little factories with different parts that have different functions that add up into an organic whole. ✓
4. According to **miasma theory**, diseases were caused by foul-smelling airs that came from rotting meats, swamps, and other putrefying sources. ✓
5. **Louis Pasteur** is credited as the co-founder of **microbiology** and the founder of **zymology**, the science of **fermentation**. This is the biological and chemical process needed to make **beer**, **wine**, **yoghurt**, **cheese**, **bread**, and lots of other foods. ✓
6. Pasteurisation is heating liquids to 60-100°C to kill microbes while preserving taste and texture. ✓
7. Related to this idea of killing bad germs, Pasteur also helped develop **hygienic** medical practices. ✓
8. Pasteur showed that no germs would grow in sealed, sterilised long-necked flasks, but microbes would appear in open flasks, proving life comes from life (bio-genesis). ✓
9. If you could identify a microorganism, you could give someone a weakened version of it, or **vaccine**, and the patient taking the vaccine would develop **immunity** to the real bug. ✓
10. (1) Microorganism must always be present in every case; (2) must be isolated and grown in culture; (3) culture samples must cause disease in healthy animal; (4) germ must be isolated from that new animal. ✓
11. **Jacques Loeb**'s work with **sea urchins** can be seen as an early form of **bio-engineering**. ✓
