## 25. Genetics, Lost and Found
1. **Gregor Mendel**'s study of English peas determined that the peas' traits seemed to be inherited **independently** of each other, which made traits useful for doing **quantitative**, or **measurement-based**, biology. ✓
2. **What did Mendel conclude from his seven-year study?** ✓
    - He noticed some characteristics were passed down often (dominant) while others disappeared for a generation (recessive).
    - He found that crosses resulted in a specific ratio: 1 in 4 were purebred recessive, 2 in 4 were hybrids, and 1 in 4 were purebred dominant.
3. **What are the three general claims that are now known as the Laws of Mendelian Inheritance? Name and describe each.** ✓
    - **Law of Segregation**: The genes that control traits are distinct.
    - **Law of Independent Assortment**: Genes for different traits switch around independently when organisms breed.
    - **Law of Dominance**: Some traits are dominant and others are recessive.
4. Mendel has created **a quantitative genetics**. ✓
5. "**Mendelism**", or the science of **heredity**, was soon renamed **genetics**. ✓
6. Retroactively, Mendel became the "**father**" of genetics. ✓
7. **What are genetic linkage maps? Why were they useful?** ✓
    - They are ways of finding the locations of genes on chromosomes.
    - They were useful for working out how traits are passed down by acting as markers of the order of genes.
8. The "Fly Boys" reconstructing their flies into a standard type that could be crossed with stable mutants became the first real **model organism**, a living laboratory **technology** that could be shared with distant colleagues, upgraded to surpass rivals, customised on demand, and re-made easily in case of emergency. ✓
9. **What was Barbara McClintock's contribution to the field of genetics?** ✓
    - She discovered how genes combine and exchange information during cell division.
    - She produced the first genetic map for corn (maize).
    - She discovered the transposition of genes (also known as "jumping genes"), which is the ability of genes to change positions on chromosomes.
    - She worked out how genes are responsible for turning physical characteristics on and off.
## 26. Thermodynamics
1. **Thermodynamics** is the physics of **heat**, **temperature**, **energy**, and **work**. ✓
2. **Count Rumford** conducted a lot of experiments in the barrels of **cannons**. He noticed that certain materials **insulated** better than others, and that **air** seemed to be involved in the **transfer of heat** ... and concluded that air is a great insulator. ✓
3. **Nicolas Sadi Carnot**'s work with **steam engines** led him to think a lot about thermodynamics. ✓
4. **What is the Carnot Cycle? What does it describe?** ✓
    - It is the science of what happens inside heat-producing engines.
    - It describes the upper limit of the efficiency of a model thermodynamic system.
5. **What is the Second Law of Thermodynamics?** ✓
    - It states that the total entropy in a closed system can never decrease; it can only stay steady or increase.
    - It means that heat cannot randomly flow from a colder point to a hotter one.
6. **Entropy** is the state of **disorder** in a system. ✓
7. The **First Law of Thermodynamics**, otherwise known as the **conservation of energy**, states that energy can **change** from one form to another, but energy is **not lost**; it has to go somewhere. ✓
8. Heat is just **motion** and vice versa. The **Joule** is the unit of energy. ✓
9. **James Joule** **forced water** through a perforated cylinder, measuring the very slight degree to which the water heated up, and found that his **mechanical heating-up energy** was the same as his electrical-heating up, about **four joules**. ✓
10. The energy of the universe is **constant**. The entropy of the universe tends to a **maximum**. ✓
11. Thermodynamics deeply **united** chemistry and physics. ✓
12. The second law of thermodynamics **preceded** the first by more than 25 years. ✓
## 27. Electricity
1. **Static electricity** is the electrical charge produced by stationary friction. ✓
2. **What is current? How does it happen?** ✓
    - Current is electricity as a flow of electrical charge.
    - It happens either by the movement of negatively charged subatomic particles called electrons through wires or by the movement of charged molecules called ions.
3. **Luigi Galvani** theorised the existence of **animal electricity**, or the electrical basis of **nerve** impulses. ✓
4. Alessandro Volta created the first practical method of **generating** electricity—the first **battery**, known as the **voltaic pile**. ✓
5. **What is Ørsted's Law?** ✓
    - It states that electric currents produce circular magnetic fields when they flow through wires.
6. **What is Ampère's Law?** ✓
    - It describes how parallel electrified wires attract if currents flow the same way and repel if they flow in opposite directions.
    - It states the force is proportional to current intensity and inversely proportional to the distance between the wires.
7. **What is the equation for Ohm's Law?** ✓
    - I = V / R
8. Electromagnetic induction means the generation of electricity in one wire via the **changing magnetic field** created by the current in another wire. ✓
9. By connecting **light**, electricity, and **magnetism**, **James Clerk Maxwell** laid the groundwork for modern **physics**. ✓
10. **Thomas Edison** was the person who first saw the potential for an entire **electrical grid**. ✓
11. **What was the Current War?** ✓
    - It was an intense public battle over the safety and efficiency of Edison's direct current (DC) versus Westinghouse’s alternating current (AC).
