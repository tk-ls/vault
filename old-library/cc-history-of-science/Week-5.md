## 13. The New Astronomy
1. Tycho Brahe believed in a **geo-heliocentric** cosmos. In this model, the **sun orbits the earth**, but the other planets **revolve around the sun**. ✓
2. Tycho observed and took **precise measurements** of the same sky all the time, noting that sometimes "stars" streaked around—these were **comets**. In 1572, he saw a new star which we now call a **supernova**. ✓
3. What were the contributions of Johannes Kepler? How was he connected to Tycho? ✓
Kepler was Tycho's assistant and collaborator. After Tycho's death, Kepler used Tycho's observational data to develop the three laws of planetary motion, explaining how planets move in elliptical orbits around the sun.
4. Kepler calculated many versions of **Mars**'s orbit using an **equant point**: an imaginary point in space that **Copernicus** had already figured out how to get rid of. ✓
5. He threw out his earlier models and tried an **elliptical** or **ovoid** orbit with the **sun** in the centre. ✓
6. What was Kepler's first law of planetary motion? ✓
Every planet has an elliptical orbit, with the sun at one of the two foci of the ellipse, not its centre.
7. What was Kepler's second law of planetary motion? ✓
Even though the speed at which a planet revolves around the sun will vary, you can figure out a constant area speed (the area described by lines drawn from the planet to the sun at different times).
8. This was important for showing that planets do actually move at **non-uniform** speeds. ✓
9. What was Kepler's third law of planetary motion? ✓
It explains the relationship between the distance from planets to the Sun and their orbital periods (the harmony of the "music of the spheres").
10. Kepler rejoiced at the **harmony of ideas**: his faith, empirical data, and elegant maths—all in **sync**. ✓
11. What were the contributions of Galileo Galilei? ✓
Built and refined telescopes; made observations of the moon, Jupiter's moons, Venus, Saturn, and Neptune; argued publicly for Copernicanism; wrote works that helped establish the scientific method.
## 14. The Scientific Methods
1. In 1609, Galileo Galilei built and refined **telescopes**, which eventually made him famous. Telescopes are a good example of how **scientific instruments** change the nature of **scientific practice**. ✓
2. Science, he said, was simply the **uncovering of God's work**. ✓
3. What data did Galileo use to argue for Copernicanism over the Aristotelian-Ptolemaic system? ✓
The phases of Venus (which appears to grow larger and smaller like Earth's moon) and the tides (whose movements seemed to prove that the earth moves).
4. Galileo's last text was a mathematical treatise about how bodies fall through the air and how wooden beams break; it was also a record of the **process** by which he discovered these **physical laws**. ✓
5. He called for **specific tests** that would let experimenters confirm his laws with their own **senses**. This, in his words, was the mark of a "true scientist": **independent confirmation**. ✓
6. Francis Bacon is famous for a new approach to science, which was **practical**, **instrumental**, and **supported by the state**. ✓
7. He believed that natural philosophers should help improve the well-being of humanity through **technological advances**. ✓
8. For Bacon, science also required **central planning** and state support. He thought natural philosophy should be a program, or system, that worked for the **public good**. ✓
9. René Descartes came up with a whole new **cosmology**, based on Copernicus, that featured a chaotic, rapidly moving aethereal fluid in which the planets and stars were **suspended** instead of perfectly crystalline spheres. ✓
10. His question, "how do we know what we know?", is at the heart of the philosophical discipline of **epistemology**. ✓
11. For Descartes, the universe is composed only of things that **maths can describe**. The tactic Descartes used for checking validity of your own knowledge is still famous today: **systematic doubting**. ✓
## 15. The New Anatomy
1. Andreas Vesalius's book, De Humani Corporis Fabrica, was a new way of doing **medicine**—a new **paradigm**. ✓
2. What did the seven books of "Fabrica" cover? ✓
The bones and cartilage, ligaments and muscles, veins and arteries, nerves, heart, brain, and organs of nutrition and generation.
3. Vesalius also wrote that the **brain** and **nerves** are the centre of the mind. This **overturned** the Aristotelian idea that the heart was the centre of thought and feeling. ✓
4. Vesalius was able to correct a lot of these misconceptions, thanks to the big innovation he used in his lectures at Padua: **dissection**. ✓
5. "Fabrica" shows how important **instruments** are to scientific knowledge-making. It was a guide for seeing the body **anatomically** instead of **humorally**. ✓
6. William Harvey is most famous for his theory of **blood circulation**. He did this specifically through **vivisection**, or cutting open live animals. ✓
7. Harvey helped bring the idea of **rigorous measurement** to medical **investigation**. ✓
8. Harvey figured out that blood flows in **one direction** at a time, thanks to **valves**. ✓
9. Anton Philips van Leeuwenhoek, the inventor of the **microscope**, became the so-called "father" of **microbiology**. Using his invention, van Leeuwenhoek discovered an entire world of amazing forms in **miniature**. ✓
10. In 1677, van Leeuwenhoek observed **spermatozoa**. And in 1682, he observed **muscle fibres**. ✓
11. What were the contributions of Jan Swammerdam? ✓
He delicately dissected animal and plant tissues under a microscope, observed how insects change from egg to larva to pupa to adult, experimented on muscle contraction, and was the first to observe red blood cells in 1658.
12. Robert Hooke's "Micrographia" was the first instance of "**cell**" describing a single chamber within living tissue. ✓
