## 1. intro to history of science
1. the word "systematically" means that there are **rules** about observing or experimenting—rules that **anyone** can follow. ✓
2. "nullius in verba" means "**on no one's word**". ✓
3. "society's philosophical transactions" is one of the world's oldest **peer-reviewed scientific journals**. ✓
4. "science" is a **historical** and **social** concept—not one that's existed forever, in the same way for all people. ✓
5. each knowledge-world system has its own **social norms** about what count as **valid** ways to make and share **knowledge**. ✓
6. around the world, humans are still **actively working** to understand our universe, but they **don't all agree** on how to do it. ✓
7. at its limit, the history of science touches on the study of **religion**: the diverse and changing **nature** of the never-ending human **search** for truth. ✓
8. one goal of this course is to highlight how the **values** (beliefs about right and wrong) and **ethics** (acceptable behaviours) of scientists and engineers **shape** our world. and how, conversely, sciences and technologies are shaped by the societies that **produce** them. ✓
## 2. the presocratic
1. the Greeks practiced **natural philosophy**, meaning self-conscious **inquiry** into nature. ✓
2. they came up with **theories** that tried to account for why stuff is the way that it is. ✓
3. schools of thought were groups of **teachers** and **students** who thought about the **same problems**. ✓
4. to convince people they were right, a natural philosopher had to use **reason, logic**, and **observation** to attack the wrong-seeming theories of others. ✓
5. the first European natural philosopher whose ideas survived down to the present was **Thales**, the first individual known to have proved a mathematical theorem—**Thales's theorem**. ✓
6. **Anaximander** is thought to have been the first European philosopher to **write down** his own ideas. ✓
7. the last great thinker associated with the Milesians was **Empedocles**. he put forward the theory of the four classical elements: **earth**, **air**, **fire**, and **water**, mixed by two forces: **love** and **strife**. ✓
8. we think of Pythagoras as having introduced the notion of **idealism** to science: idealists generated **abstract models** of perfect stuff. ✓
9. the thing that Pythagoras added to science is the role of the **mathematical proof**. ✓
## 3. Plato and aristotle
1. Socrates studied **politics** and **morality** and prided himself on not claiming to know things. ✓
2. the Socratic method is an example of **negative hypothesis elimination**, or proving that something is wrong to narrow down the possibilities of what might be right. ✓
3. Plato's best-known works include **Republic**, in which Socrates defines justice and argues for rule by philosopher-king instead of democracy, and **Timaeus**, in which Socrates talks about the nature of the universe. ✓
4. Plato had a big impact on **thinking about thinking**. he placed his own philosophy on **geometrical laws**. ✓
5. compared to Plato's idealistic abstractions, aristotle's philosophy makes more **common sense**. his ideas are based on **empirical evidence**; he observed the world and then came up with a theory that explained it. ✓
6. aristotle's influence on alexander "the great" reminds us that science is **always social**. ✓
7. he posited a **complete system**, joining the **elements** and the **heavens**. this became the basis for **European thought** about the physical world for two thousand years. ✓
8. aristotle trusted that knowledge proceeded from the experience of the **senses**. he also tried to classify the world in an orderly system, giving rise to **taxonomy**. ✓
9. Plato's **transcendental** ideas about the cosmos—even if wrong in their particulars—inspired centuries of scholars to think about the universe as having **underlying laws** ones that hold regardless of what our senses can show us. ✓
