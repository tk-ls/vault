## 19. Biology Before Darwin
1. During the **Scientific Revolution** and **Enlightenment**, there was **no biology**—that term was first used in 1799. Instead, there was **natural history**, the observation-based study of **living things**. ✓
2. **Joannes Baptista van Helmont** really wanted to understand how **plants grow**, so he devised his famous **willow** **tree** experiment to provide some answers. ✓
3. Van Helmont concluded that the tree grew not by eating soil but by **drinking water**. ✓
4. What was **Maria Sibylla Merian**'s contribution to biology? ✓
She was the leading entomologist (insect expert) of her day and a highly skilled scientific illustrator. She became well known for her work on how insects metamorphose (change shape), and her careful observations of the butterfly life cycle became a benchmark for other natural historians.
5. **Carl Linnaeus** invented the **binomial system** that biologists use today. The first name, or **genus**, represents a more general category. The second name, or **species**, is based on specific characteristics. ✓
6. What are examples of **kingdoms**, **classes**, and **orders**, respectively? ✓
Kingdoms: animal, vegetable, mineral
Classes: mammal, bird
Orders: eels, spiny-finned fishes
7. **Sir Joseph Banks** pushed the powerful British Empire to make **tabulating nature** a project of **prestige**. ✓
8. Why do we remember **Jean-Baptiste Lamarck** as the "almost-Darwin"? ✓
He developed a specific theory of "transformisme" to describe how species changed, providing a how and why for evolution.
9. Some historians still credit him with essentially **predicting epigenetics**, or changes in living things made by changes to which genes are **expressed** rather than by changing the genetic **code** itself. ✓
10. Georges Cuvier added a different wrinkle to the theory of biological transformation: **extinction**. ✓
11. What is **catastrophism**? ✓
The idea that major changes in species come about due to catastrophic events (such as the Flood of Noah). Cuvier argued that each epoch of earth history had its own distinctive flora and fauna, and these epochs were separated by global catastrophes.
## 20. Earth Science
1. If you're looking for the foundations of **geology**, one place to start is with **mining**. ✓
2. Since at least the seventeenth century, people have compared **layers of rock** and declared them younger or older, depending on their **positions relative** to other layers. ✓
3. By the late seventeenth century, some European naturalists, such as polymath **Robert Hooke**, argued that natural objects, like **fossils**, should also be used like historical texts to shed light on early human environments. ✓
4. Who were the "**Transformist**" natural historians? What did they do? ✓
Buffon, Lamarck, and Cuvier were the Transformist natural historians who developed proto-evolutionary theories and raised questions about whether species changed over time.
5. The **Comte de Buffon** argued that the earth was progressively **cooling**. According to him, during this process, the earth underwent phases or "**epochs**", which were roughly parallel with the six Biblical days of Creation from **Genesis**. ✓
6. The Genesis metaphor could be read as more of a **metaphor** that **complemented**, rather than contradicted, scientific evidence. ✓
7. What is the theory of **catastrophism**? What does it claim? ✓
Catastrophism is the theory that major changes in species come about due to catastrophic events such as tsunamis, meteorites, or earthquakes. According to this theory, life forms did not persist after the catastrophes—they went extinct with the end of their worlds. Fossils reveal a linear sequence that can be used to reconstruct the earth's past.
8. **Charles Lyell** instead argued for a slow, "**steady-state/uniformitarianism**" theory of geological changed that would become known as **uniformitarianism**. ✓
9. What were Lyell's two arguments against the French natural historians? ✓
First, Lyell thought the French had relied too much on catastrophes to explain geological history. He argued that observable geological processes (like erosion and deposition) were the only explanations geologists should consider reasonable or scientific.
Second, Lyell argued that extinctions were spread evenly across geological time—not clustered together in mass-extinction events.
10. What is "**deep time**" or "**geological time**"? ✓
The notion that, before humans, the earth had already been around for an unimaginably long time. This great expanse of time is full of sudden events and patterns that persist over epochs.
11. A **thematic map** is a map showing something about the Earth other than its shape. ✓
## 21. The Industrial Revolution
1. One reason that allowed the **Industrial Revolution** to take off in England was **social stability**—a period of **peace** followed the unification of **England** and **Scotland**. Both enjoyed a strong **rule of law** and a free market. ✓
2. Another reason was a **population boom**. ✓
3. Technologically speaking, the Industrial Revolution happened thanks to **coal**. ✓
4. The **steam engine** is a reminder that revolutionary technology often isn't one new invention, but a process of **improving** existing ones. ✓
5. How did **Thomas Newcomen**'s "**atmospheric engine**" work? ✓
It worked by using a boiler to heat the air inside a cylinder. A valve then sprayed cold water into the cylinder, creating steam and a partial vacuum, which pulled a piston down through the cylinder. The process repeated, moving the piston up and down, which also moved an attached beam that pumped water up from the mine.
6. The nineteenth century was the century of **engineering**. One critical development in **engineering** was **precision manufacturing**. This enabled the production of interchangeable parts at **scale**. ✓
7. Communication was also transformed for many people by the development of **telegraphy**, or sending messages over long distances using **electrical signals**. ✓
8. By 1800, the capital generated by cottage industries became the **foundation for factories**. And factories offered lots of advantages over a rural cottage—namely, the production could be **mechanized** and **centralized**, to make things **more quickly** for **less money**. ✓
9. How did labor change as industrialization took off? ✓
Labor went from being seasonally based to being based on clock time. Factory work started early in the morning and stopped late at night. Laborers worked in shifts and were fined if they didn't keep pace.
10. As a result of all these changes in the labor force, the whole idea of **class** also changed. ✓
11. The "**Anthropocene**" is the new epoch which means "the age of man". ✓
