## 4. India
1. **India** presents a convenient counterpoint to **Greece** because **knowledge-making** in India was inseparable from a long **religious** tradition, sponsored by the **state**, and focused on **applications**. ✓
2. the most important **Hindu** texts were the **Vedas**. they were written in a sacred language, **Sanskrit**, which was standardised around the time of the first Greek natural philosophers. ✓
3. the first science in India was **linguistics**. and this tradition of memorising the Vedas and trying to understand words eventually led to the study of **acoustics** and **musical tones**. ✓
4. how did the **Maurya empire/dynasty** begin? 
~~Chandragupta Maurya led a coup in the kingdom of Magadha in 321 BCE, inspired by Alexander the great's model and coached by a brilliant older adviser. he then conquered weaker kingdoms one by one, forging them into the Maurya empire.~~
**Chandragupta Maurya became emperor of nearly the entire subcontinent of in Eastern India; Maurya was inspired by the model of Alexander and was coached by a brilliant older adviser and led a coup in Magadha; from there he conquered the weaker kingdoms one by one and forged them into a powerful state.**
5. Indian states developed whole **government** departments to supervise the building and maintenance of **irrigation** systems. ✓
6. to control nature, the people running these big states needed to know lots of things about the **lands, plants, animals**, and especially **rivers** they controlled. and most especially about the people who **owed them taxes**. ✓
7. another key to running a big state in India was the **elephant**. ✓
8. a debate about the relative merits of **applied** versus **pure** science—knowledge of the immediately **useful** versus the abstractly **true**—is still raging today. ✓
9. **astronomy** was important to all of the ancient states. ✓
10. what is **ayurveda**? how did it begin? how did it evolve? ✓
ayurveda, literally "life-knowledge" or the science of life, began with oral traditions about sacrificial animals. by the sixth century BCE, it was a standardized system of medicine and way of answering the question "what is life?" ayurvedic approaches to diseases and cures were rational, based on medical judgment from years of study and experience.
11. Indian **medicine** and **surgery** was probably the most **advanced** of any contemporary ancient civilization. ✓
## 5. Americas and time keeping
1. the classical civilisations of **Mesoamerica** is what is now **Mexico** and **central America**. ✓
2. the earliest writing comes from the **Olmecs**, who lived in today's southern Mexico from 1500-400 BC. ✓
3. what were some of their features/contributions? ✓
they carved colossal human heads from volcanic stone, invented a mathematics including the number zero, and created a calendar system that influenced later Mesoamerican civilisations. they also developed a writing system and an art style.
4. the **Maya** built great step pyramids. these were **temples** devoted to **kings** as well as sites for making **astronomical** observations. ✓
5. they had a **base-twenty** or **vigesimal mathematical system** that included zero, but no fractions. they made a lot of **calculations** for **religious** reasons. ✓
6. what do you find most interesting about their calendar system? explain. ✓
(student's personal response - the Maya used five interlocking calendars of different lengths, including the 260-day tzolkin and the 365-day vague year, which together formed the 52-year calendar round. they also kept the long count calendar that reckoned time in millions of years.)
7. they developed a writing system of hundreds of **glyphs** depicting natural elements such as jaguars, fish, and people. these carry both **symbolic** and **phonetic** meanings. ✓
8. they also practiced intensive cultivation of **crops** such as maize, sunflower, cotton, chiles, chocolate, and vanilla using **irrigation**. ✓
9. **lidar** is a good example of how modern science can help us understand history, including the history of science. ✓
10. what caused the collapse of the maya? explain. ✓
the fragility of the Mayan food system probably played a role. deforestation to make lime for stucco may have changed rainfall patterns, leading to famines. Mayan culture came under stress in CE 800, and after 1500, Spanish genocide definitively crushed high Mayan culture.
11. the **Aztecs** (or Nahuas) were the great builders of central Mexico. they created a system of **canals**, **floodgates**, and **aqueducts**. ✓
12. the most famous **Incan** site is **Machu Picchu**, in what is now **Peru**. they used a sophisticated system of tying strings of knots, called **Khipu**, to **keep records**. ✓
## 6. roman engineering
1. the romans inherited much of their knowledge from the **Greeks**. the romans would continue to spread classical Greek thought: we even call their culture "**Greco-Roman**." ✓
2. today, we remember the romans for their **engineering**—or ability to improve some real-world system—not their deep thoughts about why the world is the way it is. ✓
3. in the ancient Mediterranean, the job of building war machines was called **architecton**, or architect. ✓
4. what were some of the contributions of **Archimedes of Syracuse**? 
~~he worked out many geometrical proofs including the area of a circle, and pioneered infinitesimals and exponents. he invented the water screw and compound pulley, and designed various war machines.~~
**fought for the Greeks against the Romans; mathematician; worked out many geometrical proofs including the area of a circle; pioneered infinitesimals and exponents; invented water screw and compound pulley; designed various war machines**
5. how does "useful" knowledge differ from "theoretical" knowledge? what were their respective names in this time period? ✓
useful knowledge was called **technē** (technology/art) - something learned by doing and seen in the real world. theoretical knowledge was called **epistēmē** (epistemology) - abstract knowledge represented by formulas, associated with science.
6. **Ptolemy's** version of the **cosmos**, a mathematically neater version of **aristotle's** and **Plato's**, became the basis of the understanding of the **universe** across much of the medieval Christian and Islamic world. Ptolemy was also pretty much the authority on earthly **geography** in the Greco-Roman world. ✓
7. the romans found out that **arches** support more weight than **straight joints**. thus the romans were able to move water long distances using arch-y **aqueducts**. ✓
8. the most important feat of roman engineering may have been their **highways** (or roads). ✓
9. what were some physical elements of roman road construction? ✓
they used cement over layers of fitted stones and gravel. drainage ditches lined the sides, and roads were cambered to allow water to drain off.
10. perhaps the most important technology the romans optimized was the **state** itself. ✓
11. one in three people in roman Italy were **enslaved** (or slaves). ✓
