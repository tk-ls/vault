## 10. Alchemy
1. Real alchemists were skilled **experimentalists**, backed by theories of **matter** ✓
2. The word "**alchemy**", which is where we get the word" **chemistry**" from, is a bit of a mystery. Alchemy is defined as a way of thinking **philosophically** about stuff by **changing** it. ✓
3. Alchemists typically encoded their philosophies in complicated **allegories**, or stories wherein the characters and actions **stand for** something other than what they appear. ✓
4. A lot of alchemical books focused on **transmutation**, or changing metals into other metals. ✓
5. Regarding agents of transmutation, how did "**particulars**" differ from "**universals**"? **Particulars only did one thing (like changing copper into silver), while universals (the philosopher's stone) could change any base metal into gold** ✓
6. The quest for the universal transmutation agent was called **chrysopoeia**, or literally, "make into gold". ✓
7. The **human body** was understood as an alchemical workshop: chemical reactions happened in the **organs**, transmuting one kind of stuff into another. ✓
8. What were some different types of alchemy in medieval Eurasia? **Chinese alchemy (tied to earth energy, feng shui, gunpowder, waidan/immortality), Indian alchemy (focused on medicine, mercury, preserving health), and Islamicate alchemy (blended Aristotelian, Chinese, and Indian practices)** ✓
9. Alchemy, like other knowledge-making systems, was torn between **text** and **experiment**—that is, between loyalty to **tradition** and **iconoclasm**, and a return to basic observation. ✓
10. Alchemy became increasingly seen as **dirty**, **dangerous**, **unsavoury**, **low-class**, and lacking a classical pedigree. Publicly, alchemy was attacked as **superstition**. ✓
## 11. Cathedrals and Universities
1. "**The Medieval**" was a "**middle age**" that lasted from roughly **CE 500 to 1400**. ✓
2. What were **cathedrals**? **Great stone churches that were the seat of a bishop and the administrative, spiritual, and educational centre of the bishopric/diocese** ✓
3. Once a cathedral was there, a city grew **economically**. ✓
4. What made cathedrals such technical wonders? **Their height, flying buttresses that distributed weight and allowed tall narrow structures, stained glass windows, and innovative architecture that used less stone while allowing more light** ✓
5. The **Black Death** was a **plague** that swept across Europe from 1348 to 1350, perhaps spread by **flea-ridden** merchants travelling the **Silk Road**. ✓
6. **Charlemagne** and his successors centralised **knowledge production** at the **palace** (at Aachen). ✓
7. Although they all feature impressive old buildings today, medieval European **universities** started off as **self-governing associations** of people with a common **function**. ✓
8. The Latin word "universitas" means **corporation**. ✓
9. How did the "**Northern Model**" universities differ from the "**Southern Model**" universities? **Northern model (like Paris) focused on theology and was an association of masters/teachers. Southern model (like Montpellier and Italian universities) focused on medicine and law and was an association of students who paid teachers' salaries** ✓
10. Increasingly, the faculty, thinking **systematically** about thinking as separate from the **Bible**, ran **afoul** of the Church. ✓
11. On the one hand, the **suppression/condemnation** of Aristotle's ideas sounds bad. But on the other, this condemnation **freed up** medieval thinkers in continental Europe to look beyond the "masters". ✓
## 12. The Scientific Revolution
1. **Historiography** is how historians have told history differently over time. ✓
2. **Thomas Kuhn** argued that different sciences undergo "revolutions" when scientists gather enough **data** that they can't explain using their current **paradigm**, or unstated, world-organising **theory** about how the universe works. ✓
3. **Anomalies** are things that the paradigm can't explain. ✓
4. Who was **Nicole Oresme**? What were his contributions to the history of science? **French scholar (born ~1320) who argued for heliocentrism 166 years before Copernicus, criticised astrology, pioneered mathematical graphs to describe motion through space over time, and worked on physics of falling objects (scooping Galileo by over a century)** ✓
5. **Nicolaus Copernicus** proposed a **heliocentric universe/cosmos**: in this model, the Earth rotates on its **axis** once every twenty-four hours, and the Earth **revolves** around the sun once every year. ✓
6. Why was Copernicus' book about revolutions so revolutionary? **It made retrograde motion disappear, dictated a definite order of the planets, and made the universe 20 times wider than Ptolemy's model - it was "pleasing to the mind" and people paid attention to it** ✓
7. Copernicus used **Ptolemy's** fifteen-hundred-year-old data to build his system. ✓
8. The **Scientific Revolution** is sometimes positioned as a break in Europe between a **Christian** concept of knowledge and a **secular**, or worldly, one. ✓
9. The "battles" about this took place in the halls of **universities** or between the covers of **books** that most people couldn't even read. ✓
10. The concept of the Scientific Revolution comes from the **nineteenth** century. ✓
