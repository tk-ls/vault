## 28. Ford, Cars, and a New Revolution
1. The first **Industrial Revolution** ramped up around 1800. It started in **Britain**, ran on **steam**, **trains**, and **factories**, and led to lots of scientific discoveries by individual researchers. ✓
2. What caused/comprised the **Second Industrial Revolution**? ✓
    - The industrialisation of electricity and mass manufacturing caused/comprised it. It happened around 1900, started in the United States.
3. One of the most iconic, scale-shifting, nineteenth-century American inventions was the **cheap automobile**, and the new, more efficient **factory system** that created it. ✓
4. **Henry Ford** pushed his team, and in 1908, they released the **Model T**, and this car **changed the world**. ✓
5. Large production numbers highlight the phrase most often associated with Ford: **mass production**. This style of production meant **specialisation** and **standardisation**. Plants used Ford's special business technique, the **assembly line**. ✓
6. What is "**Fordism**"? ✓
    - Fordism is the philosophy of building sophistication into the product, making it modular using standardised parts, and taking skill out of the work involved.
7. Ford wanted to educate his workers to become **model citizens**. ✓
8. What conditions did his workers have to meet to earn a new wage? ✓
    - They had to work for six months, be clean-living (sober, thrifty, possess good moral habits, and be involved in civic life), and swear off all involvement with union activity.
9. What were short-term effects of scaling up the auto industry? ✓
    - Scaling up infrastructure of roads, gas stations, and mechanic shops; increased access to national parks; more people moving to cities; generation of new interest in nature; research on scientific management of forests; influence on the discipline of ecology.
10. Workers came together, forming **unions**, in order to negotiate for **better labor conditions**. ✓
11. What are the **Third** and **Fourth Industrial Revolutions**? ✓
    - The Third Industrial Revolution is tied to digital information technologies. The Fourth (current) is tied to Internet-connected automation.
## 29. Cinema, Radio, and Television
1. It wasn't until the invention of commercial **sound recorders** and **motion picture cameras**, in the late 1800s, that you could consume the **same media** as other people around the globe. ✓
2. **Thomas Edison** developed the **phonograph**, which literally means "sound writing", in 1877. ✓
3. How did the "recording cylinder" work? ✓
    - It worked by vibrating a thin membrane wrapped around the cylinder, and then amplifying those vibrations (making them louder).
4. Edison was the self-proclaimed **Napoleon of technology**. ✓
5. Film movie cameras work by taking lots and lots of photographs called **frames**. When they're played back quickly, they give the **illusion of motion**, because the human brain can only process so many images per second before it just gives up. ✓
6. Edison had the **patent** on the camera, so he kept suing other movie makers on the east coast. ✓
7. Radio came **decades after** cinema. ✓
8. Radio waves have to travel long distances without **losing fidelity**, or **accuracy**. Whereas movies were just carted around using **physical reels** of film. ✓
9. Today we measure the frequencies of electromagnetic waves in units called **hertz**. ✓
10. By 1899, **Guglielmo Marconi** sent the first **wireless telegraph signal** across the **English Channel**. ✓
11. Radio also became a tool of **political propaganda** and an indispensable way of communicating important **news**. ✓
12. How did **John Baird** invent a mechanical TV in the early 1920s? ✓
    - He used transparent rods to transmit images of only thirty lines at a time (pretty low resolution).
## 30. The Mind/Brain
1. In the past, what theories explained "madness"? ✓
    - Divine punishment, possession by spirits, or an imbalance of the humours.
2. The **Industrial Revolution** gave rise to the **therapeutic asylum**, or mental hospital aimed at helping—and studying—the mentally ill. ✓
3. **Philippe Pinel**'s generation of asylum doctors marked the beginning of a shift in thought from madness to a **medical condition of the mind**. ✓
4. **Ivan Pavlov** focused on **conditioned reflexes**; he taught dogs to **associate** the sound of a metronome with being fed, causing them to salivate when presented with the sound alone. ✓
5. Pavlov's stimulus-response work became foundational to the school of psychology called **behaviourism**. ✓
6. How does **structuralism** differ from **functionalism**? ✓
    - Structuralism tries to understand things by seeing how their parts fit together, regardless of what they do. Functionalism tries to understand things by working out the purpose for them.
7. Back in the nineteenth century, **hysteria** was a way of describing various problems, including loss of motor control, paralysis, unexplained fears, fainting, **emotional outbursts**, and a host of other ailments. It was a way to describe **women** with independent ideas. ✓
8. **Sigmund Freud** started working with **talk therapy/hysterical patients** and realized that many hysterical patients were smart and otherwise "normal". ✓
9. What was psychoanalysis? ✓
    - A new form of therapy based on talking about early childhood experiences, relationships with parents, early sexual encounters, and dreams.
10. Freud also opened up the study of **sexuality**. ✓
11. According to Freud, how do the id, ego, and superego differ? ✓
    - The id is the unconscious drives, deep-seated fears and desires. The ego is the waking, conscious mental interface with reality. The superego is the mind's internalised censor and the voice of society, religion, and moral norms.
12. "Shell shock" was later rethought of as **post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)**. ✓
