## 16. The Colombian Exchange
1. Why hadn't European explorers sought out the New World prior to 1400? ✓
European states had been too small and poor to support large navies, and they didn't have technologies that other Eurasian cultures had. 
2. There was a philosophical difference in exploration between Portugal and China: most people living around the Indian Ocean respected overlapping political boundaries. But European states didn't recognise other states' claims to their own territories. ✓
3. The later colonial powers, the English, Dutch, and French, followed the Portuguese and Spanish pattern of state support for science, focused on geography and botany. Science became a tool of empire. ✓
4. These voyages were about exploitation. ✓
5. What kinds of things were exchanged as Europeans sailed farther? ✓
Plants, animals, people, diseases, ideas, and technologies.
6. Carl Linnaeus came up with a rational system for classifying plants based on their sex parts. ✓
7. In the end, empire turned out to be very uneconomical for the states themselves. ✓
8. As more people learned about the new world, they wanted to make more sense of it. So exploration led to the development of museums and the practice of scientific collecting.. ✓
9. In addition to the international violence perpetrated against native populations, disease played a major role in changing who lived there. ✓
10. What is variolation? How does it differ from vaccination? ✓
A way of protecting someone against smallpox by deliberately infecting them with material from another person who had survived the disease. How does it differ from vaccination? Vaccination uses a similar but milder virus to trick the body into fighting smallpox.
11. Alongside the "new science" created by the thinkers of the scientific revolution, the age of exploration created a new sense of the new/novelty in Europe. ✓
## 17. Newton and Leibniz
1. What is calculus? ✓
The mathematics that describes how a thing changes instantaneously.
2. What did Isaac Newton accomplish during his "miracle year"? ✓
Discovered the laws of gravity, laid down core ideas for calculus, and started developing the theory of light and colours.
3. Newton was obsessed with alchemy, or thinking philosophically about stuff by changing it. ✓
4. In what became known as his "crucial experiment", Newton showed that light is composed of rays of different colours that can be split using a prism, and that these rays can't be further split. And that the colour of light can be brought back to white using a mirror. ✓
5. Through his work on optics, Newton also developed the first functional reflecting telescope, using a mirror to focus light. ✓
6. What were Newton's "queries"? What did they do? What were their topics? ✓
Rhetorical statements meant to guide further research. Influence science for centuries. Nature and transmission of heat, possible cause of gravity, electricity, how God created matter, proper way to do science, and ethical conduct of human beings.
7. "Principia" was made up of three books. It began with axioms, or core principles. Book one focused on the motion of bodies in free space. Book two concerned the movement of bodies in a restricted medium like a fluid. Book three, finally, turned to celestial mechanics. ✓
8. What were some areas of natural philosophy in which Gottfried Wilhelm von Leibniz worked? ✓
Almost every area, reshaping libraries, inventing the mechanical calculator, creating binary notation, becoming a major figure in philosophy.
9. Leibniz used integral calculus for the first time in history to find the area under the graph of a function. ✓
10. A priority dispute/rivalry between thinkers from different nations meant that Leibniz never really got the credit/recognition he deserved. ✓
11. Scientific societies functioned as publishers. ✓
## 18. The New Chemistry
1. What was the Enlightenment? ✓
A shift in ideas about knowledge, away from traditional sources of authority like the Church, and toward scientific rationality.
2. This was an "Age of Reason". The Enlightenment was also about social values, such as individual liberty, the progress of civilisation, and religious tolerance, including the separation of church and state. ✓
3. Statistics was developed at this time to serve the interests of nation-states and early corporations. So we can also call this the Age of Empire. ✓
4. No object better represents the Enlightenment than the ambitious book named the Encyclopédie. ✓
5. What three big things did this demonstrate? ✓
Knowledge is cumulative, knowledge is recordable, and knowledge is political. 
6. Enlightenment thinkers increasingly dreamed of quantification, or describing things numerically, like units of length, mass, heat, and so on. ✓
7. What did natural philosophers believe caused chemical reactions in the late 1700s? ✓
Phlogiston, a colourless, odourless ether released during combustion.
8. The person who changed chemistry from a qualitative discipline to a quantitative one was Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier. ✓
9. He began his chemical research with the hypothesis that, during combustion, something is taken out of air rather than put into it. That hypothesis turned out to be correct, and that something turned out to be oxygen. ✓
10. Working carefully over years, he generated the first modern list of elements/chemical elements. ✓
11. How was Lavoisier so successful at setting up the new chemistry as a paradigm? ✓
He had support from Marie-Anne Pierrette Paulze (Madame Lavoisier), who translated his texts and published his complete papers after his death. Also, his students learned only the new chemistry, isolating phlogiston believers.
