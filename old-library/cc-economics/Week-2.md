## 5. Macroeconomics 10/10
1. Macroeconomics is the study of the **entire economy** as a whole rather than individual markets. Macroeconomists make **predictions** based on data, theoretical models, and historical trends. ⁂
2. What are the three economic goals of policy makers? ⁂
	- Keep the economy growing over time  
	- Limit unemployment  
	- Keep prices stable
3. The three specific measurements that economists analyze to see if a country is achieving each goal: **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)**, unemployment rate, and the **inflation rate**. ⁂
4. GDP is the **value of all final goods and services produced within a country’s borders in a specific period of time, usually a year**. ⁂
5. What are examples of transactions that would not be included in GDP? ⁂ 
	- Buying a used domestic car  
	- Buying financial assets like stocks  
	- One company buying another company (e.g. Google buying YouTube)  
	- Illegal activity (underground economy)  
	- Household production (e.g. fixing your own hot water heater)
6. **Real GDP** is GDP adjusted for inflation; nominal GDP is not. ⁂
7. A recession is defined as when two successive quarters or **six months** show a decrease in real GDP. A **depression** is a severe recession. ⁂
8. The **unemployment rate** represents the number of people that are actively looking for a job but just can't find one. People who were looking for a job but gave up are called **discouraged workers**. Frictional unemployment is when people are **temporarily unemployed or between jobs**. Unemployment caused by a lack of demand for a worker's specific type of labour is known as **structural unemployment**. Cyclical unemployment is unemployment due to a **recession or downturn in the economy**. ⁂
9. How does inflation differ from deflation? ⁂
	- Inflation is an increase in average prices over time, reducing the purchasing power of money.  
	- Deflation is a decrease in average prices, which can discourage spending and worsen economic downturns.
10. The process of economy booms and busts is called the **business cycle**. ⁂
## 6. Productivity and Growth 9/9
1. **GDP** is the market value of all goods and services newly produced in a country in one year. ⁂
2. GDP per capita is the GDP of the country divided by its **population**. ⁂
3. The United Nation's **Human Development Index (HDI)** measures life expectancy, literacy, education, quality of life, and it ranks countries according to their findings. Because data shows that countries with high GDP per capita have less infant mortality, poverty, and preventable diseases, economists often use GDP per capita to measure a country's **standard of living**. ⁂
4. Economists argue that the main reason some countries are rich is because of their **productivity** (their ability to produce more output per worker per hour). ⁂
5. What makes some countries more productive than others? ⁂
    - The quantity and quality of factors of production: land (natural resources), labor (workers), capital (machines, factories, infrastructure)  
    - Human capital: the education, knowledge, and skills of workers  
    - Technology and organizational effectiveness: how resources are combined and used to produce output
6. What is human capital? ⁂
    - Human capital is the workers' education, knowledge, and skills required to produce goods and services.
7. Technology (in economics) is defined as the sum total of **knowledge** that society has acquired concerning the use of resources to produce goods and services. ⁂
8. What computer technology change led to the US productivity boom in the mid 1990s? ⁂
    - The rise of networked computers connected via the World Wide Web, enabling communication between computers at stores, warehouses, and factories which improved organizational effectiveness and productivity.
9. In the big picture, increasing productivity has resulted in increased standards of living for much of humanity over the last **hundred years**. ⁂
