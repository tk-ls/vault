## 7. Inflation 11/11
1. Purchasing power is the amount of physical goods and services **that can be bought** by a given amount of money. ⁂
2. What is a bubble? ⁂
     **A bubble is when the price of a good soars beyond its actual value due to collective delusions and irrational exuberance.**
3. The **CPI** shows how prices have changed between different years and is the most commonly used **measure of inflation**. ⁂
4. How do nominal prices differ from real prices? ⁂
     **Nominal prices haven’t been adjusted for inflation, while real prices have been.**
5. Government economists use **adjustment factors** to simultaneously account for technological progress and keep years comparable. ⁂
6. What are some potential causes of inflation? ⁂
     **Demand-pull inflation (too much money chasing too few goods) and cost-push inflation (increased production costs like oil).**
7. Why does Venezuela have so much inflation today? ⁂
     **Economic mismanagement, reduced oil exports, and the government printing more money have led to skyrocketing prices.**
8. What caused the housing bubble in the 2000s? ⁂
     **Low interest rates, deceptive lending practices, easy credit, and speculation drove up home demand and prices.**
9. Speculation is trading a financial instrument involving **high risk** in **expectation** of significant returns. ⁂
10. What is the problem with a bubble? ⁂
     **It depends on an endless supply of new buyers—when that runs out, the bubble bursts and prices crash.**
11. What other historical bubbles have occurred? ⁂
     **The Dot-com bubble in the late 1990s and Dutch Tulip Mania in the 1630s.**
## 8. Fiscal Policy 10/10
1. Recessionary gap is a situation wherein the real GDP is **less** than the potential GDP at the full employment level. Inflationary gap is the amount by which the actual gross domestic product **exceeds** potential full-employment GDP. ⁂
2. What are some of the problems caused by recessionary and inflationary gaps? ⁂
     **Recessionary gaps cause high unemployment, leading to social issues like increased suicide and domestic violence. Inflationary gaps cause rising prices that can wipe out savings and trigger unrest.**
3. **Fiscal policy** is a way a government adjusts its spending levels and tax rates to monitor and influence a nation's economy. In the United States, that's the job of **Congress** and the **President**. ⁂
4. How does expansionary fiscal policy differ from contractionary fiscal policy? ⁂
     **Expansionary policy increases government spending or cuts taxes to stimulate the economy, while contractionary policy reduces spending or raises taxes to cool down inflation.** 
5. Classical theories assumed that the economy will **fix itself**, and that government intervention will at best lead to unintended consequences and at worst cause massive inflation and debt. ⁂
6. **John Maynard Keynes** was one of the most influential and controversial economists of the 20th century. He argued that **government spending** can make up for a decrease in consumer spending. ⁂
7. What's the harm in government spending? ⁂
     **It can increase national debt and potentially cause crowding out, where government borrowing raises interest rates and reduces private investment.**
8. Do you think the 2009 U.S. government stimulus program was a good idea? Explain. ⁂
     **Most economists think it prevented a worse recession. The U.S. recovered faster than countries that implemented austerity, like those in the Euro-zone.**
9. What is the multiplier effect? ⁂
     **It's when government spending leads to increased consumer spending in a ripple effect, so that $100 of spending might result in $175 or more of total economic activity.**
10. When people are miserable and unemployed, they want to feel like help is on the way. Doing nothing doesn't create the kind of **confidence** that will get consumers and businesses spending again, and it doesn't get politicians re-elected. So it looks like Keyne's policies are here to **stay**. ⁂
## 9. Deficits & Debts 10/10
1. How is a country's budget deficit different from its debt? ⁂
     **A deficit is when a government spends more than it collects in tax revenue in a year. Debt is the total accumulation of past deficits.**
2. The **United States** has the largest debt at a little over **18 trillion** dollars. **Note: this number is likely higher now since the video was made in 2015.** ⁂
3. Our GDP grows every year due to population growth and productivity increases and our ability to **sustain debt** grows along with our income. ⁂
4. What's driving the huge deficits in the upcoming decades? Be specific. ⁂
     **Primarily spending on Social Security and healthcare, especially for aging Baby Boomers, which is expected to grow significantly.**
5. What could prevent the government from continuing to borrow in the future? ⁂
     **A lack of willing lenders or lenders demanding higher interest rates due to fears the government can’t repay its debt.**
6. A government becoming unable to pay its bills is an example of **default**. The investors who loaned the government money lose billions, the government loses all **credibility**, and it causes a massive **recession**. ⁂
7. What are some countries that have defaulted previously in history? ⁂
     **Greece, Argentina (2001), Russia (1998), and others.**
8. The **debt ceiling** is the limit on the amount of national debt that can be issued by the U.S. Treasury. The debt ceiling, by itself though, does nothing to **cut spending** or **raise revenue**. ⁂
9. What is the good news for the United States, regarding its national debt? ⁂
     **It borrows at extremely low interest rates, and healthcare cost growth might be slowing, improving future budget outlooks.**
10. Looking at the debt from the past or even the present is a good way to have political arguments, but it may not be a great way to think about the **future**. ⁂
