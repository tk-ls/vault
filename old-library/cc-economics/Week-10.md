## 28. labour markets
1. what is voluntary exchange?  
    voluntary exchange is when both the worker and the employer agree on a wage that benefits them both. the worker provides labour, the employer provides payment, and both sides feel better off. ✓
2. in the case of stan at the pretzel shop, the wage offered has to cover his **opportunity cost**—the value of his lost **free time** and the money he could be making **doing something else**. the demand for labour depends on the demand for the products a business sells—economists call this **derived demand**. ✓
3. what are some reasons why wages in a labour market don't always end up at a competitive equilibrium?  ✓
    wages may not be at equilibrium because of discrimination based on things like race, gender, or age. they can also be distorted by monopsony power when only one employer dominates. unions and minimum wage laws also influence wages.
4. what are some situations when wages might actually be higher than equilibrium?  ✓
    wages can be above equilibrium when companies pay efficiency wages to boost productivity and reduce turnover. unions can also push wages above equilibrium through collective bargaining.
5. some classical economists argue against nearly all forms of government **manipulation** in competitive markets. they say the minimum wage not only leads to **unemployment**, but it actually hurts the people it claims to help. ✓
6. what is the logic behind this claim?  ✓
    the idea is that if wages are forced above the market level, employers will hire fewer unskilled workers. instead of earning low wages, those workers may end up with no jobs at all.
7. how do some other economists argue their support for a minimum wage?  ✓
    they argue that real labour markets aren’t perfectly competitive. employers usually have more bargaining power than individual workers, so minimum wage laws act as a correction to prevent exploitation.
8. do you support or oppose a minimum wage? explain your answer. if you support a minimum wage, what do you believe the wage should be?  ✓
    this depends on personal opinion. one possible view is supporting a minimum wage because it ensures workers earn a basic living standard. it could be adjusted by region so wages match the local cost of living.
9. very few economists argue a higher minimum wage will end **poverty**, but some argue that it could **reduce** poverty. ✓
## 29. the economics of healthcare
1. how do private insurance programs differ from public insurance programs?  ✓
    private insurance is paid through premiums collected by insurers from individuals or employers. public insurance is funded by taxpayers and run by the government.
2. what is Canada's healthcare system like? explain to the best of your ability.  ✓
    Canada has a single payer system where the government funds healthcare through taxes. most doctors’ offices are private businesses but are paid by the government, while hospitals are publicly owned and staffed. people still need private or supplemental insurance for things like prescriptions, eyeglasses, and dental care.
3. what is the united states' healthcare system like today?  ✓
    the us has a mixed system. most providers are private, and most people under 65 use private insurance. Medicare covers seniors, Medicaid covers low-income households, and the VA covers veterans. overall, it’s a patchwork of private and public systems.
4. economists evaluate the effectiveness of a healthcare system through the following three criteria: **access, cost, and quality**. ✓
5. what are some reasons the united states spend so much more money on healthcare per capita than other countries?  ✓
    Americans tend to consume a lot of medical tests and treatments, administrative costs are higher because of many insurers and paperwork, and most importantly, providers charge much higher prices compared to other countries.
6. the U.S. consumes a pretty high quantity of **tests** and **treatments** per person. U.S. **administrative costs** are also higher since a lot of countries drastically reduce their billing paperwork with a universal insurer. most of the difference comes from the fact that U.S. providers are **paid much higher prices** than their counterparts in other countries. ✓
7. the **iron triangle** refers to the mutually beneficial relationship between members of congress, government bureaucrats, and lobbyists. they all end up working toward policies that maintain the **status quo**, and aren't necessarily in the best interest of the people. ✓
8. what did the affordable care act (Obamacare) accomplish?  ✓
    it expanded health coverage by requiring insurers to cover pre-existing conditions, charge the same premiums for people of the same age, and insure everyone who applies. it also mandated that everyone get health insurance or pay a fee and provided subsidies for those who couldn’t afford coverage.
9. Obamacare reflects the people's attitude towards government and capitalism: Americans **don’t fully trust either one of them**. ✓
## 30. the economics of death
1. according to the united nations, the global average life expectancy has risen from **65 years** for people born between 1990 and 1995 to **70 years** for those born between 2010 and 2015. ✓
2. a recent study found that **income levels** affect life expectancy. ✓
3. longer average lives can boost the economy since people consume more goods and services, but they also raise healthcare costs, especially from age-related illnesses like dementia and alzheimer’s. ✓
4. surveys indicate that the vast majority of people would prefer to die **at home**, but more than 70 percent of americans die in **nursing homes** or **hospitals**. ✓
5. studies indicate that many doctors and patients are uncomfortable with the idea of having **cost-of-care** factor into **end-of-life** decisions. ✓
6. in 1982, the united states passed a law that allowed **Medicare** to cover hospice benefits for patients with **terminal illnesses**. ✓
7. funeral expenses include service fees, transportation, embalming, body preparation, use of facilities, staff costs, hearse rental, printing memorial cards, caskets, burial plots, and headstones. ✓
8. the government reduces costs by regulating funeral industry pricing through the FTC, covering hospice care through Medicare, and reimbursing doctors for discussing end-of-life planning with patients. ✓
9. individuals can reduce funeral costs by planning ahead, buying life insurance, writing a will, choosing cremation instead of burial, or simplifying services. ✓
10. when you die, your debts are generally paid from your estate. if the estate can’t cover them, creditors often lose out—relatives are usually not legally responsible, though collectors may still try to pressure them. ✓
11. the least you can do is have a **will** to guide the distribution of your **assets**. a good **financial planner** can let you in on all the ways you can move your money to the heirs you really love, while avoiding expenses like **probate court** and **estate taxes**. ✓
## 31. taxes
1. historical examples include Mesopotamians paying taxes with livestock and labour, ancient Egyptians using tax collectors and shelters, taxes in the bible, and more recent cases like Britain’s molasses and stamp acts that fuelled the American revolution, plus India’s salt march against British taxes. ✓
2. at the most basic level, taxes raise money for **government services**. taxes are used to promote the **well-being** of society. ✓
3. taxes can be used as a way to **redistribute wealth** in a society—from people who have more to people who have less. ✓
4. redistribution can happen through progressive income taxes, subsidies, vouchers like food stamps, luxury taxes, or programs like housing support. ✓
5. direct taxes are paid straight to the government by a person or business, like income or property tax. indirect taxes are collected by sellers (like sales tax or vat), and passed on to the government, so consumers ultimately pay them. ✓
6. regressive taxes take a larger percentage from lower incomes (like sales taxes). progressive taxes place a heavier burden on higher incomes (like U.S. income tax). proportional taxes charge the same percentage regardless of income (like a flat tax). ✓
7. a **flat tax** is an example of a proportional tax. economists who oppose the flat tax say that **it’s not truly simple or fair**. they argue a flat tax isn’t as **equitable** or as **straightforward** as it seems. ✓
8. there are economists who argue that the progressive income tax in the U.S. **offsets** some of our other, more regressive taxes. they say a flat tax would shift the total **tax burden** away from the wealthy to the lower and middle classes. ✓
9. after the seven years war, Britain tried taxing colonists through the molasses act (1764) and the stamp act (1765). these angered colonists, leading to “no taxation without representation,” the Boston tea party, and eventually the American revolution. ✓
10. historically, people have avoided taxes by fleeing, joining monasteries (in Europe and China), or finding loopholes. monks and Buddhists often avoided taxation, and tax resistance has fuelled protests and rebellions like Gandhi’s salt march. ✓
