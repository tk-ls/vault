## 32. the underground economy
1. for economists, the underground economy is the informal economy that is all the trade and work that happens off the books, under the table, outside of the sight and reach of **government rules and taxes.** ✓
2. what are some examples of common day activities that fit this description? ✓
    cutting hair without a license, selling fruit on the street, babysitting, selling lemonade, nanny or housekeeper paid off the books.
3. what are some indirect macroeconomic ways economists have come up with to try to estimate the informal economy? ✓
    comparing total income reported vs. total expenditures, studying demand for cash, analyzing electricity usage.
4. the underground economy is less active in **developed** countries, where economists estimate it's between **10 to 20 percent** of total GDP. in the U.S., the underground economy is probably worth somewhere between **1 to 2 trillion** dollars. ✓
5. according to the un, **organized crime** is an $870 billion/year business. about 1.5% of global GDP. **drug trafficking** is the biggest chunk of that. ✓
6. what are some other examples of illegal activity that contribute to these numbers? ✓
    counterfeiting, human trafficking, timber trafficking, ivory and horn trade.
7. because illegal markets are by their nature illegal, they can often be **dangerous** places to buy or sell. some ethicists and economists argue that the market for kidneys should be **legalized.** ✓
8. what are some risks to making the kidney market legal? ✓
    poor people being coerced into selling kidneys, exploitation of vulnerable populations.
9. what are some examples of the lack of safety-net in most underground economy jobs? ✓
    no sick time, no health insurance, no overtime pay, poor working conditions.
10. the informal economy can serve as a **temporary buffer** for individuals and the economy as a whole. ✓
## 33. economics of immigration
1. international migration is when people move **between countries** while internal migration refers to people moving around within their **home country.** ✓
2. what are some historical examples of voluntary migration to the united states?  ✓
    European migration during the Great Atlantic Migration (1840s–1910s), Asian migration to the US West Coast, Irish immigration in the 1870s, Guatemalan immigration in the 1990s, Cuban migration during the Mariel Boatlift (1980).
3. beginning in the late 19th century, a series of laws were passed to **restrict** immigration. many of these laws remained in effect until the **1960s.** when new laws helped precipitate a wave of immigration. ✓
4. the overwhelming majority of economists agree that immigration is a **good thing** for national economics. many studies indicate that increased immigration is associated with overall increases in **GDP** and **productivity.** ✓
5. what are some of the costs that come along with immigration? ✓
    low-skilled immigrants may remain poor, economic disadvantages can be passed to children, recent immigrants use more social services, short-term drops in wages, increased inequality.
6. what are some of the benefits of encouraging the immigration of high-skill workers? ✓
    high-skill immigrants innovate, contribute to patents, increase company revenue, enhance national output, provide net economic benefit.
7. a wide range of studies find that extending legal status to undocumented immigrants would be a **net positive.** proponents say that newly documented workers would gain **labor protections,** and would be free to pursue work that better matches their **skill-set.** as a result, on average, some economists estimate these workers' wages could rise up to **15%.** ✓
8. what other arguments besides economics do opponents make against immigration? ✓
    security risks, relaxed border enforcement leading to smuggling, opposition to rewarding law-breaking with citizenship.
9. the U.S. is really a **nation** of immigrants. it has the world's largest total immigration population at **41 million** and sentiment in the U.S. about immigrants is changing. young people hold a more **positive** view of the contributions than older generations, which means it's likely that **pro-immigrant** sentiments are going to keep on growing. ✓
## 34. foreign aid and remittance 
1. the average American guesses that the united states spends about **26%** of its budget on foreign aid but the actual number is only **1%.** ✓
2. foreign aid is basically the transfer of **money** or **services** from a country or international organisation, intended to help out another country and its people. ✓
3. what worthy causes is foreign aid intended for? ✓
    peace and security, democracy and human rights, economic growth, improvements to health and education systems, humanitarian assistance after disasters, protecting the environment. ✓
4. the **Marshall plan** helped legitimise and lay the groundwork for U.S. foreign aid programs that have come since.
5. what are some ways that foreign aid programs can go wrong? ✓
    lack of coordination, not involving recipient countries in allocation decisions, corruption in recipient countries, aid not reaching intended goals. ✓
6. some economists say foreign aid actually encourages **corruption**, by helping keep **bad governments in power.**
7. what have been some of the recent public health aid success stories throughout the world? ✓
    eradication of guinea worm in sub-Saharan Africa, malaria cases down by a third, malaria death rates down 60%, new HIV infections down 35%, AIDS-related deaths down nearly a quarter, 7.8 million lives saved. ✓
8. **remittances** are the money that migrants and immigrants send to their home country. according to the world bank, remittances can have a significantly positive effect on the home country, leading to a reduction in **poverty**, larger health and education expenditures, better access to **technology**, and less **child labour.** ✓
9. what are some economists' criticisms of remittances? ✓
    they can foster dependency on outside money, dis-incentivize growth in the home economy, and make it overly susceptible to the global economy.
10. how do public charities differ from private foundations? ✓
    public charities get most support from the general public and government, while private foundations are controlled by a small group of people and funded mostly by a few donors and investments, giving them more agenda control. 
## 35. economics of happiness
1. economists and psychologists have discovered a **disconnect** between how we buy and spend and do, and what actually makes us **feel** better off. they've realised that sometimes, getting paid nothing for doing something can make us feel **happier** than getting paid. ✓
2. beyond some level of income, the value of each additional dollar has **diminishing** returns in terms of our day to day well-being. ✓
3. for a long time, psychologists believed that people have a "**set point**" for happiness and that most big changes had only a "**temporary**" effect on happiness. but more recent studies show that **economic** choices and circumstances can have lasting effects on just how happy we are. ✓
4. what are some of the latest revelations from happiness research? ✓
    unemployment has a big negative effect on happiness, long commutes reduce happiness, credit card debt lowers happiness, relative income affects happiness, hours worked have diminishing returns on happiness, inflation—especially unpredictable—reduces happiness.
5. what is the reference-income hypothesis (or ranked-income hypothesis)? ✓
    it says satisfaction from income and consumption depends on how one’s income compares to others around them, not just absolute income.
6. the "**Easterlin Paradox**" finds that as the income level in a country rises, the average level of happiness in those countries doesn't always follow. ✓
7. one explanation for this is that we derive happiness from **status** rather than absolute income. ✓
8. income growth may not be the best way to mark or judge the **progress** of a society. ✓
9. in 2011, the **United Nations** adopted a resolution that calls for member states to give greater weight to happiness and well-being when figuring out how to pursue social and economic development. ✓
