## 25. Monopolies and Anti-Competitive Markets
1. A pure monopoly is a market controlled by **one seller** with a good or service that has no close **substitutes**. The true power of a monopoly comes from its ability to keep **competitors out** of the market. ✓
2. What is crony capitalism? ✓
Crony capitalism occurs when a company uses government connections and influence to create unfair market advantages rather than competing through better products or services. The transcript illustrates this with an example of someone owning all food truck licenses and convincing the mayor to ban traditional food vendors, creating a monopoly through political influence rather than market competition.
3. Crony capitalism is a big reason why many economists call for government **transparency** and **accountability**. ✓
4. An **oligopoly** is when a few firms have a large majority of market share. ✓
5. What are antitrust laws and why do they have that name? ✓
Antitrust laws are regulations that promote competition and outlaw anticompetitive tactics by monopolies. They're called "antitrust" laws because monopolies used to be called "trusts," and these laws were designed to break up or prevent the formation of such business trusts that stifled competition.
6. The **Sherman Act** outlaws any monopolization or attempted monopolization. ✓
7. How does horizontal integration differ from vertical integration? ✓
Horizontal integration is when companies buy other companies that produce similar products, like if Coca Cola tried to purchase PepsiCo. Vertical integration is when a company directly owns or controls its supply chain, such as Ford in the 1920s owning iron mines, coal mines, and making its own steel, glass, and tires.
8. Anti-trust regulations can also prevent companies from making anti-competitive deals with their **suppliers**. ✓
9. What is a patent and why is it beneficial? ✓
A patent grants an inventor exclusive rights to profit from a specific product or process, essentially guaranteeing their right to be a monopoly for a limited time (usually about 20 years). Patents encourage innovation by allowing companies like pharmaceutical firms to recover their research and development costs and earn profits before competition enters the market.
10. A **natural monopoly** is a special situation when it is more cost effective to have one large producer rather than several smaller competing firms. ✓
11. **Deregulation** is the process of removing or reducing state regulations. ✓
12. What is price discrimination? ✓
Price discrimination is the practice of charging different consumers different prices for exactly the same product. Companies accomplish this by segregating the market based on consumers' willingness to pay, such as airlines charging different prices based on booking time, with early bookers paying less than last-minute travelers.
## 26. Game Theory and Oligopoly
1. Give a brief explanation of each of the four different market types listed below: perfect competition - monopoly - monopolistic competition - oligopolies ✓
**Perfect competition** involves thousands of producers selling identical products with easy market entry, where individual businesses have no control over prices. **Monopoly** features one large company producing a product with few substitutes and high barriers preventing competition, giving significant price control. **Monopolistic competition** has many producers with relatively low barriers selling very similar but not identical products, allowing slight price variations based on consumer preferences. **Oligopolies** are markets with high barriers to entry controlled by a few large companies, where each company makes decisions considering their competitors' actions.
2. The most recognizable form of non-price competition is **advertising**. ✓
3. **Game theory** is the study of strategic decision-making. ✓
4. What is the "prisoner's dilemma"? ✓
The prisoner's dilemma is a classic game theory scenario where two people arrested separately must decide whether to confess without being able to communicate. If both confess, both pay $10,000; if neither confesses, both go free; if one confesses and the other doesn't, the confessor walks free while the other pays $20,000. Because they can't cooperate, both rationally confess and end up with the suboptimal outcome of both paying $10,000.
5. In Game Theory, even if people or companies rationally follow their own **self-interest**, the best outcome is hard to reach when they can't or don't **cooperate**. ✓
6. **Collusion** is a secret agreement or cooperation, especially for an illegal or deceitful purpose. There are strict **antitrust** laws designed to prevent it. ✓
7. **Price leadership** is when one company changes its prices, and its competitors have to decide if they're going to follow suit. ✓
8. What is a "payoff matrix"? ✓
A payoff matrix is a tool economists use to explain oligopolies and game theory by showing the profit outcomes for competing companies under different pricing scenarios. The matrix displays numbers representing potential profits for each company based on whether they choose high or low pricing strategies, helping predict optimal outcomes and dominant strategies.
9. At its best, healthy competition promotes **innovation**. ✓
## 27. Behavioural Economics
1. Behavioural econ is a subfield of economics that focuses on the **psychological**, **social**, and **emotional** factors that influence decision-making. ✓
2. What is bounded rationality? ✓
Bounded rationality refers to the limits on information, time, and abilities that might prevent people from seeking out the best possible outcome. This concept recognizes that people don't always make perfectly rational decisions due to these constraints, which can lead to behaviors that contradict classical economic theory.
3. Classic economics assumes that consumers have **perfect information** when making choices, but in reality, they often don't. ✓
4. Behavioural economics doesn't blow up traditional economic theory, it just seeks to understand why people **behave differently** than economic models suggest. ✓
5. What is the ultimatum game? ✓
The ultimatum game is a behavioral economics experiment where two players decide how to share a sum of money (like $100). The first player proposes a split, and if the second player accepts, both keep their portions; if rejected, nobody gets anything. While classical theory suggests any offer should be accepted (since something is better than nothing), unequal splits are often rejected, demonstrating that human behavior is motivated by concepts like fairness and justice, not just monetary gain.
6. If people were entirely rational they would consistently **make the same decision** when given identical options, but sometimes people's preferences are dependent on **how the options are presented**. Psychologists call this type of cognitive bias the **Framing Effect**. ✓
7. What's an example of this given in the video? ✓
The video provides several examples including whether you'd rather eat beef that's "75% fat free" or "25% fat," choosing between a raffle with "1 out of 1000 winners" versus "999 losers," or supporting the "Improve our Schools Act" versus the "Raise our Taxes Act." Each scenario can be framed differently to influence decision-making despite containing identical information.
8. **Nudge theory** is a concept which argues that nudges encourage people to act a certain way without actually changing their choices. ✓
9. How does the experiment involving the two envelopes with potential cash or the guaranteed money demonstrate a person's attitude toward risk? ✓
The envelope experiment reveals risk tolerance by offering a choice between guaranteed money ($50) or a 50/50 chance of getting $100 or nothing. If you accept exactly $50, you're risk-neutral; if you accept less than $50 to avoid the possibility of getting nothing, you're risk-averse. This demonstrates how people's attitudes toward uncertainty affect their decision-making beyond pure mathematical expected value.
10. **Loss aversion** is the idea that people strongly want to avoid losing. Studies show that, in general, losses are more **painful** than gains are **pleasurable**. ✓
