## 22. Environmental Econ 9/9
1. Carbon dioxide emissions are one of the primary greenhouse gases that basically blanket the earth and are causing climate change. CO2 levels are the highest they've been for millions of years, which is why environmentalists consider it a "planetary emergency." ⁂
2. The two steps of the economic solution are: Step one, identify the sources of the most air pollution (which are factories that burn fossil fuels for energy, industries that use oil and coal to produce things, and vehicles with internal combustion engines). Step two, decrease the supply of these technologies and products or decrease the demand for them. ⁂
3. One of the biggest problems with having countries independently enforce environmental regulations is the Tragedy of the Commons. No one owns the atmosphere, so there is very little **incentive** for countries to keep it clean or switch to expensive green **technologies** if no one else is going to. ⁂
4. Pollution represents a **market failure**, a situation where markets fail to produce the amount that society wants. ⁂
5. An example of a price incentive the government could establish would be adding taxes to gasoline purchases, or on the other hand, providing subsidies for people who drive electric cars. These incentives encourage individuals to make choices that are better for the environment. ⁂
6. Governments can also create permit markets, which set a **limit** on how much firms can pollute, and allowing those firms to buy and sell pollution **permits**. ⁂
7. The rebound effect refers to how the expected gains from energy saving technologies might be offset as people change their behavior. For example, when someone buys a more fuel-efficient car and saves money on gas, they might start driving more than they normally would or take additional trips, which leads to more consumption and possibly even more emissions. ⁂
8. **Private companies** and **governments** are also funding research into green technology. In the U.S., the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009 allocated billions to fund **renewable** energy. ⁂
9. One individual action you can take to help save the planet is conserving and consuming more thoughtfully in your daily life, such as improving insulation in your home and changing thermostats. However, the transcript notes that individual action alone isn't going to be enough and that industries, governments, and individuals need to work together. ⁂
## 23. Economics of Education 10/10
1. Education is a positive externality because it benefits individuals by helping them get a job and earn more income, but it also benefits society as these individuals create art, invent cool stuff, cure diseases, and make interesting conversation at parties. More education increases productivity, GDP, and standards of living. ⁂
2. Some economists argue that the government should pay for early education programs, and provide extra money for disadvantaged and **low-income** students. For others, the answer isn't just about more funding; it's about having more **competition**. Some economists also focus on the **teachers**, and argue that they should be **incentivized** to improve student performance. ⁂
3. The "college wage premium" is the fact that college graduates, on average, earn more than those without college degrees. Among 25-32 year-olds, college grads earn an average of $45,000 versus $28,000 for those who only have a high school diploma. ⁂
4. The fact that college graduates make more money isn't just about college; it's also about **life circumstances**. ⁂
5. The two main reasons why college graduates earn more are the "Human Capital" theory (the idea that going to college actually teaches you skills that'll help you get a higher income job) and "Signalling" (the idea that a college degree sends a clear signal about your intelligence and hard work that can't be easily faked by others). ⁂
6. More students are attending college than ever, and more of those students are paying for at least part of their education with **loans**. In 2012, almost **70 percent** of students took out loans to pay for it, and the median amount they borrowed was around **27,000 dollars**. ⁂
7. For-profit colleges and universities are contributing to college debt because students at these schools tend to take on more debt than students at public schools or private non-profits. ⁂
8. It's also possible that student debt is rising because **graduate school** enrolment is up. And grad students **borrow more** than undergrads. ⁂
9. Another reason tuitions are increasing is because the actual cost of **running a college** is higher than a few decades ago. ⁂
10. This is a personal question that would depend on individual circumstances and goals. The transcript suggests that whether college is worth it depends on where you go to school, how much you pay for your degree, what degree you get, and what you want to do with your life. ⁂
## 24. Revenue, Profits, and Price 10/10
1. Accounting profit is revenue minus just explicit costs (those traditional out-of-pocket costs you think of when you run a business), while economic profit is revenue minus both explicit and implicit costs (which includes indirect opportunity costs). For example, if a lawyer opens a pizza shop with $50,000 revenue and $20,000 explicit costs, accounting profit would be $30,000, but economic profit would be -$70,000 when factoring in the $100,000 he could have earned as a lawyer. ⁂
2. An example of an implicit cost you give up when you go to school each day could be the money you could earn doing a job, time with family and friends, or the opportunity to binge watch shows. The transcript specifically mentions giving up "the opportunity to binge watch Gilmore Girls." ⁂
3. Businesses calculate their **potential revenue** and their **costs of production**, including **implicit costs**, to make informed decisions. ⁂
4. Normal profit is what economists call zero economic profit, which is the minimum level of economic profit a company needs to stay in business. It means businesses make just as much as they would doing something else, covering all their costs including opportunity costs. ⁂
5. **Variable costs** change with the amount produced while fixed costs are **fixed** (they are the basic operating expenses of a business that cannot be avoided). The cost per unit is known as the **average cost**. ⁂
6. Economies of Scale exist when companies that produce more can utilize **mass production** techniques and spread out their fixed costs over many units. ⁂
7. The profit maximizing rule is to continue to produce as long as the marginal revenue of the last unit produced is greater than or equal to the marginal cost. This is often shortened to "produce where MR equals MC." ⁂
8. The Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns states that as you add variable resources (like workers) to a set number of fixed resources (like ovens), the additional output generated from each additional worker will eventually decrease. There are just too many cooks in the kitchen. ⁂
9. Studying is an example of this law because the returns from your first hour of studying are high (instead of failing your final exam, you may get a C), but every additional hour gives you lower and lower returns. At some point, maybe the twelfth hour of studying, your grade would actually go down since you stayed up all night and fell asleep during the test. ⁂
10. A **sunk cost** is a cost that's already been paid and can't be recovered. Economists stress that these costs shouldn't be included when making **future decisions**. ⁂
