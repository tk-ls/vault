## 19. Markets, Efficiency, and Price Signals 11/11
1. Productive efficiency means making goods at the lowest possible cost without wasting resources, while allocative efficiency means producing the goods and services that consumers actually want. ⁂
2. **Central planners** are less likely to be allocatively efficient because they have a harder time getting feedback about what people want. ⁂
3. Some economists argue against giving gifts because it often results in people receiving items they don’t value as much, which wastes resources. They see it as inefficient compared to people just buying what they truly want. ⁂
4. The ideal gift, in terms of efficiency, is **cash**. ⁂
5. Competition between businesses keeps prices down and **quality up**. ⁂
6. The United States government directly controls the markets for **national defense** and **public education**. ⁂
7. Price gouging is when sellers sharply raise prices for essential goods like food, water, or gas during emergencies. ⁂
8. Many economists say that anti-price gouging laws promote **inefficiency** and actually make the problem **worse**. ⁂
9. Below-cost pricing, also known as predatory pricing, is when a business sells products at a loss in order to drive competitors out of the market. ⁂
10. The risk is that after eliminating competitors, the company may raise prices, but this can attract new businesses to enter the market again, making it hard to sustain long-term profits. ⁂
11. Capitalism, with its system of price signals, is basically **crowdfunding**. A market-based society still has shared **social goals**. ⁂
## 20. Price Controls 10/10
1. Economists call the idea of the government setting prices **price controls**. ⁂
2. A price ceiling sets a maximum legal price below equilibrium, which can create shortages, while a price floor sets a minimum legal price above equilibrium, which can create surpluses. ⁂
3. In terms of actually helping consumers and producers, the vast majority of economists consider price controls **counter-productive**. One notable exception is **minimum wage**. ⁂
4. A historical example of a price ceiling was President Nixon’s 1970s wage and price freeze. It was popular at first but ultimately ineffective and caused shortages. Another example is Venezuela’s price ceilings on goods like food and medicine, which led to empty shelves and long lines. ⁂
5. Economists agree that price ceilings on rent **reduce** the quantity and quality of the housing that's available. ⁂
6. A subsidy is a government payment to individuals or businesses to encourage production or consumption of a specific good or service. ⁂
7. Economists don’t like farm subsidies because many farmers are already wealthy, and subsidies can discourage innovation while distorting market prices. ⁂
8. A survey of economists found that **85%** think the United States should **eliminate** agricultural subsidies. ⁂
9. A historical example of an agricultural subsidy is the Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933, which paid farmers not to grow crops and had the government buy up excess produce. ⁂
10. A common criticism of any government assistance is that it distorts markets, creating inefficiencies and unintended consequences, such as encouraging risky behaviour or overproduction. ⁂
## 21. Market Failures 11/11
1. One of the problems with the free market is that sometimes people have a **personal incentive** to do something that is against the **collective interests** of the group. ⁂
2. **Free riders** are people who benefit without paying. ⁂
3. A public good has two characteristics: **non-exclusion** and **non-rivalry**. ⁂
4. The difference: **Non-exclusion** means you can’t stop people from using it even if they don’t pay, while **non-rivalry** means one person’s use doesn’t reduce availability for others. ⁂
5. The Tragedy of the Commons is when shared resources get overused and depleted because individuals act in their own self-interest. Example: **overfishing** or **deforestation**. ⁂
6. The subfield of economics that addresses these issues is called **environmental economics**. ⁂
7. Negative externalities are costs pushed onto others (like pollution), while positive externalities are benefits given to others (like education). ⁂
8. Externalities are the **justification** for almost everything that the **government** does. ⁂
9. **Regulatory policies** are rules established by government decree. **Market-based policies** are policies designed to manipulate markets, prices, and incentives to fix failures. ⁂
10. Economists prefer market-based policies because they correct incentives while also raising revenue (like taxes on cigarettes) instead of just restricting behavior. ⁂
11. In an **unregulated** global economy, where producers want to make products as **cheaply** as possible, there's an **incentive** to ignore the environment to **get ahead**. ⁂
