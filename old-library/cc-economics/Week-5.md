## 13. Recession, Hyperinflation, and Stagflation 9/9
1. What was Germany's strategy to pay reparations after World War I? What happened as a result? ⁂
Germany's strategy was to print a lot of their currency (the Mark) to pay massive reparations to the Allies. As a result, this led to hyperinflation with higher and higher prices. By November 1923, it took a trillion marks to buy one U.S. dollar, and there were one thousand billion mark notes in circulation. The mark became effectively meaningless.
2. **Hyperinflation** is when a country experiences a monthly inflation rate of over 50% or around 13,000% annual inflation. ⁂
3. Why is hyperinflation so bad? ⁂
Hyperinflation erodes wealth - people who had saved for retirement saw their savings wiped out. It forces people to spend as quickly as possible rather than save or lend, so there's no money available to fund new businesses. The uncertainty also limits foreign investment and trade.
4. Economists call the number of times a dollar is spent per year the **velocity of money**. When people spend their money as quickly as they get it, that **increases velocity**, which pushes inflation up even faster. ⁂
5. A depression is when **real GDP falls** and keeps falling for a long period of time. When economies fall into deep recessions, there are more **workers** than there are **jobs** and more output than consumers want to buy, so both **income and prices** fall. ⁂
6. What is a liquidity trap? ⁂
A liquidity trap occurs when people expect further price declines and change their behavior in ways that work against central bank policies. If people expect prices to fall, they wait to buy things to get lower prices. This causes spending to decline along with the velocity of money, leading to further price declines and creating a vicious cycle of falling prices and expectations of lower prices.
7. What made America get out of the Great Depression? ⁂
It wasn't monetary policy that ended the Great Depression - it was the massive government spending of World War II.
8. Stagflation is when output **slows down or stops**, or stagnates, at the same time that prices rise. ⁂
9. When has the United States experienced stagflation? ⁂
The U.S. experienced stagflation starting in the 1970s, after a series of supply shocks including a rise in oil prices and a die-off of Peruvian anchovies (which were important for animal feed and fertilizers). This ended in the early 1980s when Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker cut the money supply and raised interest rates dramatically.
## 14. Economic Schools of Thought 9/9
1. Economic theories are constantly being **proven**, **disproven**, and **revised**. ⁂
2. The founder of modern economics was a Scottish philosopher named **Adam Smith**. He introduced the idea that a person following their own self-interest could end up serving the **common good**. He also advocated **free trade**. ⁂
3. What economic idea was espoused in the Communist Manifesto in 1848? ⁂
Rather than examining individual behavior, Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels looked at economic classes and argued that history was explained by the conflict between workers and property owners. This process would inevitably lead workers to overthrow their bosses, ushering in a new stateless and classless system called communism.
4. John Maynard Keynes argued that market economies don't **self-correct** quickly because prices and wages take time to adjust. He believed that during recessions it is necessary for the **government to get involved** by using monetary and fiscal policy to increase output and decrease unemployment. ⁂
5. What is socialism? ⁂
Socialism is a political and economic ideology where economies allow for private property and markets, but also have government ownership of industry, significant regulation, and big public programs like universal health care.
6. What are the beliefs of the Austrian School of economics? ⁂
The Austrian School, led by Friedrich Hayek and Ludwig von Mises, argued that heavy state involvement has never produced the results it promised, and that regulation and government tinkering is actually a problem, not a solution. They reject nearly all forms of fiscal and monetary policy, arguing the economy is too complicated to manipulate.
7. **Monetarists** focused on price stability and argue the money supply should be increased slowly and predictably to allow for steady growth. ⁂
8. What is supply-side (or trickle-down) economics? ⁂
Supply-side economics advocates deregulation and cutting taxes, especially corporate taxes, with the theory that benefits will "trickle down" through the economy.
9. What is the current trend of economic systems throughout the world? ⁂
Most countries that once supported strict communism (like China and Cuba) have moved toward capitalism, while many capitalist countries have adopted socialist-looking programs. It appears the world's economies are converging towards the middle - a blend of market capitalism with some government programs and regulation.
## 15. Imports and Exports 11/11
1. **International trade** is the lifeblood of the global economy. ⁂
2. What are some major exports of the United States? ⁂
The US exports high-tech things like pharmaceuticals, jet turbines, generators and aircraft. It also exports intellectual goods like Kanye West albums and Pixar movies as well as bulk commodities like corn, oil and cotton. 
3. The annual difference between a country's exports and imports is called **net exports**. If net exports are negative, it's called a **trade deficit**. ⁂
4. The value of international trade is that it doesn't make sense to make everything on your own if you can trade with other countries that have a **comparative advantage**. ⁂
5. What's an example of trade where what's good in the aggregate is not necessarily good for individuals? ⁂
NAFTA is an example. While economists say NAFTA had a net positive impact on all three countries and the US economy boomed in the 1990s creating millions of jobs, some workers were clearly hurt. For instance, a guy assembling TVs at a US factory was probably making a lot more at his manufacturing job before he got "reshuffled" to work at a burrito assembly line at Chipotle due to trade competition. 
6. **Protectionist** policy is placing high tariffs on imports and limiting the number of foreign goods to protect local businesses. ⁂
7. The **exchange rate** is how much your currency is worth when you trade it for another country's currency. ⁂
8. What does it mean for a currency to have appreciated? What happens with imports and exports when appreciation occurs? ⁂
When a currency has appreciated, it means each unit of that currency has gotten more powerful/valuable. When the dollar appreciates, it gets cheaper for US consumers to import foreign goods, and US exports to other countries get more expensive. US imports rise and exports fall.
9. If the value of the currency has fallen, it has **depreciated**. ⁂
10. Every country keeps an accounting statement called the **balance of payments** that records all international accounts. It's made up of two sub-accounts, the **current account** and the **financial account** (sometimes called the capital account). ⁂
11. How do these accounts differ? ⁂
The current account records the sale and purchase of goods and services, investment income earned abroad, and other transfers like donations and foreign aid. The financial account records purchases of financial assets like stocks and bonds. When Americans spend money on foreign goods, the people in those countries can either buy US goods (current account) or buy US financial assets like stocks and bonds (financial account).
