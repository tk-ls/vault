## 10. The Federal Reserve 11/11
1. The Federal Reserve is the **central bank** of the United States and it's commonly called "**The Fed**". Europe has the **European Central Bank** or ECB. ⁂
2. What are the two important jobs of central banks? ⁂
They regulate and oversee the nation’s commercial banks to ensure they have enough reserves, and they conduct monetary policy to adjust the money supply and influence the economy. 
3. An **interest rate** is the price of borrowing money. The amount lent is known as the **principal**. ⁂
4. When interest rates are **low** borrowers will borrow more and spend more. When interest rates are **high** borrowers will borrow less and spend less. ⁂
5. The two things that keep the banking system healthy are **confidence** and **liquidity**. ⁂
6. Liquid Assets are assets that can be **converted into cash** quickly and with minimal impact to the price received. ⁂
7. What are the three main ways the Federal Reserve can change the money supply? ⁂
Changing the reserve requirement, changing the discount rate, and conducting open market operations (buying or selling government bonds).
8. The option the Fed uses most often is **open market operations**. ⁂
9. How was this exemplified in the 2008 financial crisis? ⁂
The Fed bought massive amounts of government bonds to boost the money supply and dropped interest rates close to zero, later using quantitative easing to purchase longer-term assets like mortgage-backed securities.
10. Overall, how does fiscal policy differ from monetary policy? ⁂
Fiscal policy changes government spending or taxes, while monetary policy changes the money supply.
11. Many economists argue that for your garden variety fluctuations, **monetary policy** is more effective. But in a very severe downturn, **fiscal policy** might become much more effective. In 2008, the United States **used both**. It also depends on whether your country's central bank is tangled up in **politics**. ⁂
## 11. Money and Finance 10/10
1. What are the three main purposes of money? ⁂
Money serves as a medium of exchange, a store of value, and a unit of account.
2. Today, cash and coins are often used as money since they’re easy to carry around, physically durable, and **hard to counterfeit**. ⁂
3. **Bitcoin** is a virtual currency that is not issued or regulated by a specific country. No bank is involved, so people can buy things more **anonymously**. This appeals to people who don’t **trust** central banks and also people who want to buy **illegal** stuff online. ⁂
4. What makes the paper money is printed on so valuable? ⁂
In the past, paper money was backed by the gold standard, meaning each dollar could be exchanged for a specific amount of gold. Today, its value comes from confidence that people have that it has value, not from a physical backing.
5. **Lenders** are sometimes corporations with a bunch of cash, but they can also be ordinary households. **Borrowers** can be other households who want to borrow money to buy stuff like a car or a house or can be businesses that have a great idea for a new product but need **money** to make the product. ⁂
6. **Governments** sometimes need to borrow money because they’re spending more than they’re bringing in. ⁂
7. What are the three ways this exchange can take place? ⁂
Through banks, the bond market, and the stock market.
8. Stocks are known as **equity**. If a company enjoys high profits, **shareholders** get more money. ⁂
9. Why aren’t fluctuations in the stock market necessarily indicative of how the economy’s doing?
Because stock market changes often reflect real or perceived changes in economic fundamentals like consumer confidence, unemployment, and GDP growth, and can also be influenced by speculation. ⁂
10. Financial markets, with instruments like stocks or bonds, allow borrowers to crowdsource the money they need to borrow. They raise their capital from lots of investors and **spread the risk around**. ⁂
## 12. The 2008 Financial Crisis 9/9
1. In the 2000s, investors in the U.S. and abroad started throwing their money in the U.S. **housing market**. Big, global investors bought investments called mortgage-backed securities, which are created when large financial institutions buy up thousands of individual mortgages, **bundle** them together, and sell shares of that pool to **investors**. ⁂
2. What are subprime mortgages and why were they utilized?
Subprime mortgages are loans given to borrowers with poor credit or low income, often with lax lending standards. They were utilized to create more mortgages and mortgage-backed securities to meet investor demand. ⁂
3. The new lax lending requirements and low interest rates **drove housing prices higher**, which only made the mortgage backed securities and CDOs seem like an **even better investment**. ⁂
4. Try to summarize why the housing market collapsed as best as you can: ⁂
The housing bubble burst because borrowers defaulted on mortgages, home prices fell, and mortgage-backed securities lost value, leading to financial institutions and investors losing money.
5. What are some ways in which the government acted to help remedy the financial crisis?
The government bailed out banks, created TARP, pumped stimulus money into the economy, conducted stress tests, and passed financial reform. ⁂
6. In 2010, Congress passed financial reform called the **Dodd-Frank** law. It took steps to increase **transparency** and prevent banks from taking on **too much risk**. ⁂
7. A **perverse incentive** is when a policy ends up having a negative effect, opposite of what is intended. This is connected to moral hazard, which is when one person takes on more risk because someone else bears the **burden** of that risk. ⁂
8. What does “too big to fail” mean? ⁂
It means certain financial institutions are so large and interconnected that their failure would harm the entire economy, so they receive government bailouts to avoid collapse.
9. Who do you think was most to blame for the financial crisis? Why? ⁂
**Banks and lenders** for pushing sketchy loans to people who couldn’t really pay them back.
