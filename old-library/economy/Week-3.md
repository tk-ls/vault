## Chapter 9
1. What are the two things that the wise and restrained rulers focused on?
    1. ~~Protecting property rights.~~ *Encouraging business expansion*
    2. ~~Enforcing contracts.~~ *Encouraging personal savings*
2. In Chapter 8, the Constitution that was drafted kept voting to the taxpayers only. What change did some senators want to make and ultimately get it changed?
    They wanted to change the Constitution to allow all citizens to vote, not just taxpayers, which they succeeded in doing.
3. Who were the opponents competing for the senator position and what did each promise?
    A new challenger (representing the common man) promised generous social spending programs. The incumbent (representing savers/taxpayers) promised fiscal restraint and low taxes.
4. What did the new senator come up with in order to deal with the gap in his spending plan?    
    He proposed printing paper "Fish Notes" as currency to pay for the programs without raising taxes. 
5. Who gave his opinion stating that the Constitution gave the Senate no authority to take money from one citizen for the sole benefit of another?
    The Chief Judge (representing the judicial branch).
6. Were the citizens comfortable with the new paper notes?
    No, they were initially wary because the notes were not backed by real fish (savings).
7. Why was Max Goodbank VII not fond of the new fish notes?
    He knew that printing money not backed by real savings would lead to inflation and destroy the value of the savings entrusted to his bank.
8. Eventually what happened to the fish in the savings that were supposed to be accounted for the paper that were handed out?
    The government spent the fish (real savings) on consumption, leaving the Fish Notes backed by nothing.
9. What could happen in the economy if there is no safety in bank deposits?
    There would be massive bank runs and a total collapse of the banking system as people panic to withdraw their real savings.
10. What did the scientists show Goodbank that the Senators thought was a good solution to the problem of not having savings?
    ~~They showed that printing more paper notes and declaring them legal tender was a way to "stimulate" the economy without real savings.~~ *They would use discarded fish to make smaller "official fish"*
11. How did the senator plan to pass in order to keep the citizens from giving their opinion of the two different fish?
    By passing a law declaring that the Fish Notes were "as good as fish" and making them legal tender for all debts, public and private.
12. What super sneaky practice were they going to put in place so citizens wouldn't pay attention to the differences in the fish?
    ~~Fractional reserve banking, where banks only kept a fraction of deposits on hand, lending out the rest.~~ *They banned comparing official fish to the caught fish*
13. What will the government form so fishing would be in government control instead of the people controlling it?
    The Fishing Department (a state-controlled industry).
14. What did the government put in place in order to give reassurance to the depositors?
    Deposit insurance (a government guarantee that deposits were safe).
15. Ultimately, did Goodbank go along with the Senators?
    Yes, he eventually had to go along with the new regulations and the creation of paper money.
16. What was the purpose of the Fed?
    In the book's allegory, the Fed's purpose is to manage the money supply and control interest rates, specifically to fund government debt and prevent the unsustainable boom from collapsing too soon.
17. Ultimately what are we left with today?
    A currency (the dollar) that is not backed by real savings, managed by the Fed, and susceptible to inflation and economic crashes caused by malinvestment.
## Chapter 10
1. Name 2 of the benefits that the projects funded by the government provided.
    1. Created jobs (temporary).
    2. Appeased the voting public.
2. What did the government offer the workers of the Fishing Department?
    A guaranteed job, government benefits, and a guaranteed supply of fish (wages).
3. What was the result of the Fishing Department not working efficiently?
    The department was highly inefficient, producing fewer and lower quality fish than private fishermen.
4. What was the result of the official fish becoming 50% smaller than real fish?
    The government decreed that the 50% smaller fish was still worth 100% of the old fish, which is the mechanism of inflation.
5. Define "fishflation".
    A term used in the book to describe the loss of purchasing power of the official fish currency due to the government increasing the supply of fish (currency) without a corresponding increase in real wealth.
6. What did the government continue to do because of having a "blank check"?
    They continued to spend and print money/fish notes to fund their programs without restraint.
7. What result came from this?
    An economic boom based on printed money (malinvestment) followed by eventual high inflation and a crash.
8. What happened when the islanders finally noticed the fish they were withdrawing were smaller than the ones they deposited?
    They realized they were being cheated; they started to lose faith in the currency and saved less.
9. Who did this affect the most?
    Savers and fixed-income earners.
10. Who did the islanders start relying on because he gave the best hope of overcoming the losses?
    The "brilliant economist," Brent Barnacle.
11. What were the 2 results of the Senate trying to make jobs more secure by setting stricter limits on companies?
    1. Companies became **less efficient/productive**.
    2. Companies had **less incentive to hire** or innovate.
12. Rising prices are merely the **symptom** of inflation.
13. When inflation is present in a recession, prices may **rise** or **fall less** than they would have with no inflation.
14. What NEEDS to happen during a recession in order to rebalance the economy?
    Malinvestments must be liquidated (allowed to fail), prices and wages must fall, and resources must be reallocated back into productive uses.
15. How do governments fight recession?
    By increasing spending, lowering interest rates, and printing money (measures which the book argues prevent the necessary rebalancing).
16. When few people are working, what happens?
    Production decreases, leading to less real wealth and a lower standard of living.
## Chapter 11
1. What did the "brilliant economist", Brent Barnacle, want to do about the problem of no fish left in the bank?
    He proposed a plan of "Quantitative Easing" (printing more money) to provide liquidity and encourage spending.
2. Describe what it was like if you were living as a citizen of Sinopia.
    Citizens lived in a centrally planned economy where the King controlled all production and told people what to do; they had little freedom and low living standards.
3. What was the punishment for those who did not sing the songs well while they fished?
    They were beaten or punished by the King’s guards.
4. How many fish per day did the average Sinopian get?
    ~~One fish per day.~~ *Half a fish a day*
5. What did the king of Sinopia believe was the key to advancement in the economy?
    Military power and conquest.
6. Explain what was wrong with the senators exchanging fish Reserve Notes for real fish with the island of Sinopia?
    The Senators were giving away real wealth (real fish) to Sinopia in exchange for worthless paper Reserve Notes that represented fish that didn't exist in the Bongobian banks.
7. What was the best thing for the Sinopians to do with the fish notes?
    Trade them back to Bongobia for real capital goods (like fishing nets and tools) or real fish.
8. What two things were a result of the new transactions coming in by the Sinopians?
    1. The Sinopians rapidly **built up their capital base** (infrastructure, factories, etc.).
    2. Bongobia rapidly **consumed its capital base** (outsourcing production and living off credit).
9. What was the reversal in policy the king made with regard to the nets?
    He shifted from discouraging the use of nets to encouraging their production (investing in capital goods) because he saw the Bongobians getting rich from them.
10. Describe the relationship between the United States and China.
    The U.S. (Bongobia/Usonia) imports more goods from China (Sinopia) than it exports, paying with dollars (Notes) which China holds, thus financing U.S. consumption and Chinese production.
11. True or False. The U.S. dollar is accepted as the exchange currency for any international transaction.
    True.
12. If the U.S. didn't have savings from other nations, what would be the result?
    The U.S. would not be able to finance its massive trade deficits, and the dollar would depreciate rapidly, leading to a sharp decrease in U.S. consumption.
13. Why would it be easier for our relationship with China to end earlier?
    China (Sinopia) has less political incentive to maintain the status quo and could quickly decide to stop accepting dollars for goods, triggering a crisis.
