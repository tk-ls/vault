**1. Describe Trivers' observations of striped cleaner fish.**
Trivers observed that cleaner fish have a mutualistic relationship with larger "client" fish. The cleaner fish eat parasites from the client's body and even inside its mouth, benefiting both parties. Crucially, the client fish could easily eat the cleaner but refrains from doing so. This demonstrates reciprocal altruism—the client benefits from parasite removal and "repays" the cleaner by not eating it. Trivers noted that cleaners operate from fixed locations, which helps maintain the relationship since clients can return to the same cleaner repeatedly, making cheating (eating the cleaner) a poor long-term strategy.
**2. What does Dawkins explain about the song of the saddle-back bird of New Zealand?**
Dawkins uses the saddle-back as an example of cultural transmission (memes) in animals. These birds have distinct song dialects that vary by region. Young birds learn their songs from neighbouring adults rather than inheriting them genetically. The songs are passed down through imitation, making them memes rather than genes. Dawkins notes that new song variations can arise and spread through the population, demonstrating memetic evolution.
**3. How do memes spread, according to Dawkins' discussion?**
Memes spread through imitation and communication. When one person learns an idea, tune, phrase, fashion, or technique from another, the meme has replicated by jumping from one brain to another. Memes can spread through speech, writing, teaching, and other forms of cultural transmission. Like genes, successful memes are those that are good at getting themselves copied—they may be catchy, useful, or emotionally compelling.
**4. Explain Dawkins' idea of a "meme pool."**
The meme pool is analogous to the gene pool. Just as genes exist in a shared pool from which organisms draw their genetic inheritance, memes exist in a shared cultural environment (books, conversations, media, traditions) from which minds draw ideas. Memes in the meme pool compete for attention and "brain space." Those with high longevity, fecundity (ability to spread), and copying-fidelity tend to dominate the meme pool over time.
