**1. How does Dawkins say that DNA differs from blueprints for making a building?**

DNA differs from building blueprints in a crucial way: there is no "architect." While building blueprints are created by a conscious designer with a specific plan, DNA instructions arose through natural selection without any conscious planning or designer. Additionally, DNA is more complex than a simple blueprint—genes aren't exact "pages" but can be sentences, half-pages, or multiple pages long, and can even be split across different locations or out of order.

*Dawkins says that DNA differs from blueprints for making a building because DNA gives plans for making different proteins. The proteins, then build parts of living entities.*

**2. In Dawkins' metaphor about DNA, what is the role of page numbers?**

In Dawkins's metaphor, where DNA is compared to architect's plans in a building, the "page numbers" represent the organising structure within chromosomes. Each cell is like a room with a bookcase (the cell nucleus), containing binders (chromosomes) with pages (genes). The page numbering helps organise these genetic instructions, though unlike actual blueprints, the genetic information can be more scrambled and complex.

*In Dawkins' metaphor about DNA, page numbers mark the function of the DNA. In sexual reproduction, a new "book" may get different pages from different books, but as long as the DNA has each page, or type of DNA function, it has full instructions for a body.*

**3. Explain how the Watt Governor uses mechanics to regulate itself.**

The Watt Governor is a mechanical device that uses negative feedback to regulate the speed of a steam engine. It consists of a pair of balls that spin around as the engine runs. When the engine spins too fast, centrifugal force causes the balls to rise and move toward a horizontal position. The arms are connected to the steam valve in such a way that as the balls approach horizontal, the steam supply is reduced, slowing the engine down. If the engine slows too much, the balls drop, opening the valve more and speeding it back up. This automatic self-regulation happens without any conscious control—it's pure mechanical feedback achieving what appears to be purposeful behaviour.

*The Watt Governor uses a mechanical process to regulate itself by creating a reaction when the arms move too fast. Force from the movement pushes up heavy balls to close a valve, slowing the arms.*

**4. How do genes directly and indirectly control behaviour?**

Genes cannot directly control behaviour in real-time because of time lags between the gene's "instructions" and the need for immediate responses. Instead, genes control behaviour indirectly by building brains and nervous systems that can make quick decisions based on circumstances. Genes program general strategies and rules rather than specific responses—like a computer programmer who writes code but doesn't directly control every action the computer takes. The brain then uses these programmed strategies to simulate outcomes and choose behaviours that maximise gene survival.

*Genes must directly control behaviour when behaviour results from the ways that proteins are formed, in simple organisms. In more complex organisms, genetic control of behaviour is indirect. Genes control the proteins that create a thinking brain, which in turn controls behaviour.*

**5. What does Dawkins say about how genes "teach" a behaviour to a bee?**

Genes don't directly "teach" bees behaviours through conscious instruction. Instead, they create the neural wiring and behavioural programs that result in specific actions. In the foul brood example, genes code for proteins that influence the development of the bee's nervous system in ways that make certain behaviours likely—such as detecting diseased larvae and removing them. The gene might work by, for example, giving bees a taste for infected wax caps, making the eating of those caps rewarding, which leads to repeated uncapping behaviour. The behaviour emerges from the genetic programming, not from conscious learning or teaching.

*Dawkins says that we don't know how a gene "teaches" a behaviour to a bee, or results in that behaviour. It may affect the tastes or preferences of the bee, or there may be other means by which the gene causes the bee to behave in a certain way.*

**6. What are the main actions living organisms do, as gene machines?**

As gene machines, living organisms primarily do two things: survive long enough to reproduce, and successfully pass on their genes to the next generation. This includes acquiring resources (food, territory, mates), defending against threats, competing with rivals, sometimes cooperating with relatives or others when it benefits gene transmission, and reproducing. All behaviours evolved because they helped genes replicate themselves more successfully.

*There are two main actions living organisms do, as gene machines. They survive, and they reproduce.*

**7. Explain Dawkins' argument for why cannibalism is very rare.**

Dawkins argues that cannibalism is rare because it would not be an Evolutionary Stable Strategy (ESS). If members of a species regularly ate each other, this would create too much danger of retaliation—similar to why the "hawk" strategy is unstable in contests. When there's a contest between members of the same species, there's less asymmetry (they're equally matched) than between different species. This means that attempting to kill and eat a member of your own species is much riskier than hunting a different species that might simply flee. A cannibalistic strategy would be unstable and selected against because of the high risk of being killed in return. It's not altruistic restraint—it's selfish gene logic that makes cannibalism disadvantageous.

*Dawkins' argument for why cannibalism is very rare rests on an analysis of the benefits to the individual or aggressive behaviour towards one's own species. Based on the goal of a stable system, aggressive behaviour like cannibalism is detrimental to the individual and therefore dies out.*

**8. Explain what a prober-retaliator is.**

A prober-retaliator is a conditional strategy in evolutionary game theory. It's essentially like a regular retaliator (acts peacefully like a dove unless attacked, then fights back like a hawk) but with an important addition: it occasionally tests or "probes" its opponent with a brief aggressive escalation, like a hawk would. If the opponent fights back, the prober-retaliator immediately reverts to peaceful dove-like behaviour. However, if the opponent doesn't resist the probe, the prober-retaliator continues the aggressive behaviour and takes advantage of the weakness. This makes it nearly an ESS—it's one of the most stable strategies in computer simulations because it can defend itself, cooperate with other cooperators, but also exploit true doves.
