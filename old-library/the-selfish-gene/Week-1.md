1. Describe the comparison that Dawkins makes regarding a gangster.
Dawkins compares genes to Chicago gangsters in the sense that genes are ruthless, selfish entities that use survival machines (bodies) as their vehicles for replication, much like how gangsters use others for their own purposes. The comparison emphasises that genes are not benevolent or concerned with the welfare of the individual organism or species; they are simply focused on their own propagation. Just as a gangster operates selfishly to preserve and advance their own interests, genes operate with a kind of "selfish" logic, manipulating the organisms they inhabit to maximise their own chances of being copied into future generations.
**In the comparison that Dawkins makes regarding a gangster, he says that if you met a gangster who lived a long and successful life, you would think the gangster was tough and selfish because his world was competitive and dangerous. The same is true, Dawkins says, for plants and animals that have survived and been successful, because the natural world is competitive and dangerous.**
2. What does Dawkins say would happen if a selfish member of a species were born into a species of unselfish members?
Dawkins argues that if a selfish individual arose by mutation in a population of altruistic individuals, that selfish individual would have a significant advantage. The selfish individual would exploit the altruism of others, taking resources and benefits without reciprocating or bearing costs. This would allow the selfish individual to reproduce more successfully than the altruistic individuals. Over time, the gene for selfishness would spread through the population through natural selection, eventually replacing the genes for altruism. This demonstrates why pure, unconditional altruism is evolutionary unstable and why apparently altruistic behaviours must actually serve some selfish genetic purpose to persist.
**Dawkins says that if a selfish member of a species were born into a species of unselfish members, the selfish member would take advantage of the others. The selfish member and its offspring would be so successful that they would take over the species.**
3. What does Dawkins have to say about the phrase "to ensure the survival of the species?"
Dawkins is highly critical of the phrase "for the good of the species" or similar group selection arguments. He argues that this is a fundamental misunderstanding of how evolution works. Natural selection operates primarily at the level of genes, not species or even individual organisms. Behaviours that appear to be "for the good of the species" are actually the result of genes acting in their own selfish interests. The phrase is misleading because it suggests that organisms make sacrifices for the collective good, when in reality, any such behaviours persist only because they ultimately benefit the genes that code for them. Evolution doesn't work toward species survival as a goal; genes that happen to promote their own replication are the ones that persist.

4. Describe the behaviour of chains of atoms in human blood, as Dawkins describes it.
Dawkins describes haemoglobin molecules in human blood as intricate chains of atoms that behave in a remarkably organised and purposeful way. These molecular chains transport oxygen from the lungs to tissues throughout the body with remarkable efficiency and precision. He uses this example to illustrate that even at the molecular level, there appears to be complex, coordinated behaviour that serves a function. This complexity and apparent purposefulness in biology is not the result of conscious design but rather the product of billions of years of evolution through natural selection. The haemoglobin molecules exist in their current form because genes coding for effective oxygen transport were selected for over evolutionary time.
**Chains of atoms in human blood link up to form amino acids. These make up protein molecules that twist into even bigger molecules called haemoglobin. They are always formed in the same way because the shape is the most natural and stable.**
5. Why doesn't Dawkins find it statistically remarkable that an early molecule would arise that would copy itself?
Dawkins argues that given the vast timescales involved (millions of years) and the enormous number of molecular interactions occurring in the primordial oceans, the emergence of a self-replicating molecule was actually quite probable, even if the chance of any single molecule spontaneously becoming a replicator was extremely small. With countless trillions of molecules undergoing random chemical reactions over millions of years, even highly improbable events become virtually certain to occur eventually. Once such a replicator arose just once, it would begin copying itself, and natural selection would take over from there. The key insight is that we only needed one successful replicator to emerge once in all that time and space for life to begin.
**Dawkins doesn't find it statistically remarkable that an early molecule would arise that would copy itself because there would have been many billions of changes over billions of years for the molecule to form. The vast number of changes means that the formation of a seemingly "freak" molecule is no so remarkable.**
6. How does Dawkins view whether fidelity is important to evolution?
Dawkins views fidelity in replication as having a nuanced relationship with evolution. While some degree of copying fidelity is essential for preserving successful genetic information and allowing adaptations to be passed on, perfect fidelity would actually prevent evolution from occurring. Mistakes in copying (mutations) are the source of variation upon which natural selection acts. Without these errors, there would be no new traits for selection to favor or eliminate. So evolution requires a balance: enough fidelity to preserve beneficial adaptations, but enough imperfection to generate the variation needed for evolutionary change. Mutations are neither inherently good nor bad—they're simply necessary for the evolutionary process to function.
**Dawkins says that the answer to whether fidelity is important to evolution is tricky. Without fidelity, a creature cannot reproduce. At the same time, without mistakes, or infidelities, adaptation or change cannot occur. Ultimately, Dawkins simply says that fidelity is the tendency of things that reproduce, and mistakes, or infidelities, just happen.**
