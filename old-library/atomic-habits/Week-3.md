## Make it attractive: how to make a habit irresistable
#### What are examples of "highly engineered versions of reality that are attractive?
Things like processed snacks, fast-paced video games, and endless-scroll apps like Instagram or TikTok are all highly engineered to trigger dopamine. They amplify pleasure far beyond natural levels — like how junk food delivers way more sugar, salt, and fat than whole foods, making them hard to resist.
#### What's the importance of dopamine in habits?
Dopamine plays a key role in the *craving* part of a habit. We don’t just get a dopamine hit when we get a reward — we get one when we *anticipate* the reward. That craving is what drives us to repeat habits, even before the habit starts.
#### What is the importance of the 2nd law of habit formation?
The 2nd law, “Make it Attractive,” reminds us that if a habit feels appealing, we’re way more likely to actually do it. Habits that are unpleasant or boring won’t stick. If you design a habit to be tempting, your brain is more willing to engage.
#### What is temptation bundling and how did Ronan Byrne employ it?
Temptation bundling is pairing something you *want* to do with something you *should* do. Ronan Byrne, an engineering student, programmed his exercise bike so that Netflix would only play if he was pedaling. This way, he linked entertainment with exercise.
#### Premack's principle includes: (give two examples)
Premack’s principle is about using a more desirable activity to reinforce a less desirable one. Example 1: “You can scroll social media only after finishing homework.” Example 2: “Only drink your favorite coffee if you wake up before 7am.”
#### Give three examples of temptation bundling you can use for habits you are trying to create or habits you are trying to stop.
1. Only listen to your favorite playlist while running.  
2. Only watch YouTube while doing chores.  
3. Only eat out with friends after cooking your own meals 3 nights a week.

## The role of family and friends in shaping your habits
#### Summarise the story of the Polgar sisters and chess.
The Polgar sisters were raised by a father who believed genius could be taught. He trained them in chess from a young age and built a home environment completely centered on chess. They all became world-class players, proving that social environment shapes skills and habits deeply.
#### Give an example of imitating habits from these 3 groups.
##### Imitating the close
We mimic people around us — like picking up the studying habits of your sibling or best friend if they’re focused and disciplined.
##### Imitating the many
If most people in your school or friend group are wearing a style or using a productivity app, you’re likely to follow. The crowd sets the standard.
##### Imitating the powerful
We tend to adopt behaviors from those we admire or see as successful — like following an athlete’s morning routine or a business influencer’s reading habits.
#### How can you help enact change (in habits) with your community?
Join or build a group where your desired habit is the norm — like a book club, gym group, or study team. When a habit is seen as socially valued and you’re held accountable by others, you’re way more likely to stick to it.

## How to find and fix the causes of your bad habits
#### Change some "don't want to" statements to "get to" statements
Instead of saying “I don’t want to do this homework,” try “I get to learn and get ahead.” That small shift helps you feel empowered rather than forced, making hard tasks feel more like opportunities than chores.
#### What are some examples of motives? (give three examples)
1. To feel relaxed (often behind habits like over-watching TV).  
2. To avoid discomfort (like putting off assignments).  
3. To feel connected (like compulsively checking messages or social media).
#### Reflect on bad habits you are trying to break, what are the underlying motives?
Let’s say you keep doomscrolling at night — the motive might be to escape stress or boredom. Or if you overeat after school, maybe the real motive is comfort or coping with tiredness.
#### How does reframing a cue (thoughts around a desire) help break a habit? (write a before and after)
*Before:* “I’m tired, so I deserve junk food.”  
*After:* “I’m tired, so I need real food and rest to feel better.”  
Reframing changes the emotional response to the same trigger, which helps you make better choices.
#### How does conditioning work and how can you apply that to your life?
Conditioning is associating a behavior with a reward so your brain learns to crave it. If you always treat yourself to something small after finishing a workout or studying, your brain starts to link the habit with feeling good, making you more likely to do it again.
