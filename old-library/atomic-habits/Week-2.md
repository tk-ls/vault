## Make it obvious: the man who didn't look right
### Cues can produce automated responses, what are examples from the book that demonstrate it can be good and it can be bad?
A good example is the Japanese train conductors use “point-and-call” to trigger awareness and reduce errors. The cue (approaching a signal or task) leads to a verbal and physical response, which boosts focus and safety. A bad example is in the story of the paramedics, one of them noticed something was “off” about a patient—he didn’t look right. The other paramedics were following automated routines based on cues, but those routines didn’t fit the unusual situation, showing that automated responses can make you miss warning signs.
***Good examples include identifying when things are off, and bad examples include apologising before asking a question, accidentally cutting up a credit card, automatically turning the TV on.***
### Habit scorecard: what is point-and-call and why is it beneficial?
Point-and-call is a system where you physically point to an object or action and say out loud what you're about to do. For example, a train conductor might point at a signal and say “Signal is green.” It’s beneficial because it forces your brain to consciously engage with a cue instead of acting on autopilot. This reduces errors and increases awareness, especially in high-stakes or repetitive environments.
***Point-and-calling makes it obvious as to what you are doing. This makes you self-aware. Train operators and plane pilots do this in order to be aware of what they are doing, relay that information to necessary counterparts, and reduce the chance of an accident.***

### What is the implementation intention? Why does it help people have higher rates of carrying out an action?
An implementation intention is a plan you make beforehand about when, where, and how you'll act on a goal. It sounds like: "I will ==behavior== at ==time== in ==location==." It helps because it removes ambiguity. You’re no longer waiting to “feel motivated”—you’ve got a clear plan, which makes it way more likely that you’ll follow through. 
***Implementation intention is the pre-planned thought of carrying out an action at a specific time and place. It's not motivation that some people lack, but a clear plan of action in order to carry out their goals.***
### Give examples of "implementation intention".
“I will do 10 push-ups at 7:00 AM in my bedroom.”
“I will read 10 pages of a book at 9:30 PM on the couch.”
“I will start my homework at 4:00 PM at my desk after school.”
### Compare and contrast the Diderot effect to the implementation intention.
The Diderot Effect happens when one new purchase or habit leads to a chain reaction of more. Like buying a new outfit suddenly needing new shoes wanting a new bag, etc. It’s kind of reactive and unconscious. Implementation intention is the opposite it’s proactive and deliberate. It helps you stick to a specific plan rather than spiral into unplanned actions. 
***The Diderot effect is "habit stacking". Both implementation intention and the Diderot effect helps create habits. Diderot effect differs from the implementation intention because you simply tack on an additional act (habit) on to a habit or routine you already have.***

## Motivation is overrated; environment matters more
### How did changing the environment at the Massachusetts General Hospital improve the eating habits of its workers and visitors? 
By placing water bottles in more visible and accessible spots around the cafeteria, workers and visitors started buying more water and less soda without being told to do anything. The environment changed their behavior just by making the healthier choice easier to see and reach.
***By changing the floor plan, the hospital made those options "less obvious" and by providing bottled water at every drink station it made a healthier alternative "obvious". This decreased sales of sodas because of where they were located***
### How do businesses utilize the environment to increase their sales?  
Businesses design store layouts to trigger buying habits placing candy near the checkout, using end-cap displays, and creating relaxing atmospheres with music or scents. All of these environmental cues nudge you to spend more without even realizing it.
***Placing brand products at eye-level as well as products at the end of aisles because those areas get a lot of foot traffic.***
## The secret to self-control

### What did researcher Lee Robins find out about addiction and Vietnam War veterans?
She found that many soldiers who were addicted to heroin in Vietnam were able to quit easily once they returned home. The change in environment made it easier to break the habit, showing that addiction isn’t always permanent—it’s often tied to surroundings.
***5% of heroin addicts became re-addicted within a year of returning home. 12% became re-addicted within three years. The environment changed where the cues for heroin use were non-existent or less.***
### What is the key to people having a strong will-power of self-control?
The key is not having more willpower, but designing your environment to reduce temptation. People who appear to have strong self-control are usually just better at avoiding situations where they would be tempted in the first place.
***Changing the environment (remove the cues that give you the desire).***

### Context is the cue: summarize how you can link habits with particular environments.
You can build strong habits by pairing them with a specific place. When you always do a habit in the same spot, your brain starts to associate the environment with that behavior, making it more automatic over time. 
***Only get into bed when you are ready for bed. Use the study room for only studying.
