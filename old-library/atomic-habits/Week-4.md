## Walk slowly, but never backward
#### What surprised the professor when he had a 'quantity' group and a 'quality' group?
Although the professor thought that the 'quality' group would take better photos, in fact, the 'quantity' group did better since they had more practice, and learned through trial-and-error, while the 'quality' group spent too much time theorising than actually taking photos.
***While the quality group only had to produce one photo, their work wasn't as great as the quantity group because the quantity group got more practice instead of thinking of how to get the perfect shot.***
#### What's the difference between motion and action?
Motion is the process of planning, and preparing to do a task, and while it seems productive, it doesn't produce actual results. Action is more like a behaviour that delivers real outcomes, and actually doing the thing. Action is the thing that actually moves you towards your goal, while motion is procrastinating while looking like you're busy.
***Motion doesn't give results, action does. Outlining ideas for a project vs actually doing a project or researching the best workout programs vs working out.***
#### What is the author's response to 'how long does it take to build a new habit?'
The author says that it is the wrong question. People often think maybe 21 to 30 days, but really, there is no fixed number because habit formation depends on frequency, not time. Really, a habit is built when you do it enough times that it becomes automatic, so he suggests asking, 'how many repetitions will it take before it becomes natural?'
***It is a matter of repetition. The more times you do a habit, the more likely it is to become a habit that you automatically do.

## The law of least effort and the 2-minute rule
#### Why does the author argue that 'the less energy a habit requires, the more likely it is to occur'?
His point is that humans naturally follow the path of least resistance, meaning that if something is easy, the friction is low, so you are inclined and more likely to do it. If some habit becomes inconvenient, your brain will eventually switch to easier alternatives. Making habits require less effort means you will do them more often without forcing yourself, leading them to becoming automatic.
***If there isn't as much friction between you and a desired outcome, then putting forth the habit to achieve that out come is easier. If there is a lot of friction or effort required, people are less motivated to do the habit and stick with it.
#### What are 3 examples in the book for 'achieving more with less effort'?
From the book, examples include: 
1. Buying pre-cut vegetables so when you cook at home, you are using less effort and it takes less time to prep.
2. Using technology to automate processes such as payment and reminders, so you don't have to manually track them.
3. Laying out your workout clothes the night before so you can start exercising with no preparation in the morning.
#### What are 3 examples for priming your environment for future use?
The author includes examples such as:
1. Setting out your coffee mug, filter, beans the night before so making coffee is effortless in the morning.
2. Leaving your guitar in the middle of the living room so you are more likely to pick it up and practice.
3. Putting healthy snacks up front in the fridge so you are more likely to grab them.
#### How does progression in 'habit shaping' work if you start out with the 2-minute rule?
It says that you should start a habit by doing the easiest, shortest version, something that only takes 2 minutes or less. Once that's automatic, you can start scaling it up. 
***If you start out creating a new habit that requires so little time and effort, you are more likely to do it. If it takes so little time and effort, there is less friction to start the action. If it takes so little time and effort, the reward of having completed the action is more attainable and motivates you to do the activity. By starting off small, you can build the duration of your habit, kind of like habit stacking.***

#### How did Victor Hugo use a commitment device? How can this be utilised for those who are addicted to their phones?
He used a commitment device when he had a book deadline. He locked away all his clothes except a large shawl so he couldn't go outside and socialise. This forced him to stay indoors and write. For phone addiction, a similar strategy would be locking your phone in a timed safe, giving it to a friend to hold during work hours, or using apps that block social media entirely during set times. This removes the option to 'just check' and forces focus on other activities.
***Hugo liked hosting lavish events and spent a lot of time adorning himself and entertaining guests instead of writing. He had his assistant lock up his clothes until he finished his project. Instead of making it easy to procrastinate, he made it difficult to keep doing a bad habit. For phones, students can have friends or family hold on to their phone until their work gets done. Some products by companies have time locked commitment devices to help people stay away from their phones during certain times. By restricting a bad habit, better habits can be formed.

1. Setting up automatic savings transfers so you save money without thinking about it.
2. Using smart lights that turn on at a certain time to cue your evening routine. 
3. Pre-scheduling workouts or meal deliveries to remove decision fatigue.
