## 7. Perceiving is Believing
1. **Perceptual Set example:** The duck-rabbit image. Hank Green shows how if he cues "bird," you see a duck, but if he cues "mammal," you see a bunny. ✓
2. **Figure-ground relationship:** This worksheet demonstrates the figure-ground relationship because the text/questions are the "figure" (main focus) while the blue background is the "ground" (surroundings they stand out against). ✓
3. **Proximity:** we group **things that are near each other/nearby figures** together ✓
4. **Continuity:** we perceive smooth, **continuous patterns** rather than discontinuous ones ✓
5. **Closure:** we **fill in gaps** to create a complete, **whole object** ✓
6. **Binocular Cues - both eyes** **Monocular Cues:** depth cues available to **one eye** ✓
7. **Relative size and height** - helps us judge distance (like knowing a chihuahua is far away, not tiny) ✓
8. For Motion Perception, shrinking objects are **retreating/moving away** while enlarging objects are **approaching/coming closer** ✓
9. Your brain **constructs** your **perceptions** ✓
## 8. Consciousness
1. Consciousness is our **awareness** of ourselves and our environment. ✓
2. Six states of consciousness: ✓
    - Waking
    - Sleeping
    - Dreaming
    - Drug-induced (hallucination)
    - Meditation
    - Hypnosis
3. Cognitive Neuroscience is the study of how **brain activity** is linked with our **mental processes** (or thinking, perception, memory, language) ✓
4. **Structural imaging** shows the brain's anatomy (useful for identifying tumors, diseases, injuries), while **functional imaging** shows electromagnetic or metabolic activity like blood flow to observe correlations between mental functions and brain activity. ✓
5. **The squirrel example:** Your conscious mind says "look, a squirrel!" while your automatic mind simultaneously processes: color: brown, tail: bushy, movement: climbing, distance: 20 meters, etc. ✓
6. Selective Attention is the focusing of **consciousness/attention** on a particular stimulus or group of stimuli. ✓
## 9. To Sleep, Perchance to Dream
1. Three purposes of sleep: a. Recuperation b. **Growth/supports growth** (pituitary glands release growth hormones) c. **Memory/mental function** (improving memory, processing events, boosting creativity) ✓
2. An electroencephalograph (EEG) is a machine that measures the **brain's electrical activity** ✓
3. Sleep stage characteristics: **NREM-1:** First stage of sleep, irregular brain waves, brief/transitional, may experience hypnagogic sensations (feeling like falling), can be easily awakened; **NREM-2:** Sleep spindles (bursts of rapid brain wave activity), definitely asleep but can still be easily awakened, brief fragmentary dreams possible; **NREM-3:** Slow rolling delta waves, deep sleep, brief fragmentary dreams possible, where night terrors/sleepwalking/sleep-talking occur; **REM:** Rapid eye movement, vivid visual dreams, motor cortex active but muscles paralysed (except eyes), paradoxical sleep, brain very active ✓
4. **REM sleep is paradoxical because:** The motor cortex is very active (jumping all over the place), but the brain-stem blocks those messages, leaving muscles completely relaxed/paralysed - so the brain is highly active while the body is deeply asleep. ✓
5. A typical sleep cycle lasts **90 minutes** (or about 90 minutes) ✓
6. Sleep disorders: **Insomnia:** Persistent problems falling asleep or staying asleep; **Narcolepsy:** Brief, uncontrollable attacks of overwhelming sleepiness ("sleep attacks"), may be caused by hypocretin deficiency; **Sleep Apnea:** Temporarily stops breathing during sleep until decreased oxygen levels cause waking; **Night Terrors:** Increased heart/breathing rates, screaming, thrashing, seldom remembered upon waking; occur during NREM-3, most common in children under 7, caused by stress/fatigue ✓
7. The study of dreams is known as **oneirology** ✓
8. The information processing theory seems most valid because it's supported by research showing people recall new tasks better after REM sleep. This makes practical sense - our brains need to organize and consolidate the massive amount of information we take in daily, and dreaming appears to serve this function by sorting events and fixing them into memories. ✓
