## 18. The Growth of Knowledge

2. Schemas are mental frameworks that help interpret information. ✓
3. A toddler who has never seen a deer but has a schema for a horse calls the first deer she sees "a horsey," fitting it within her existing understanding. ✓
4. After more interactions, the child realizes deer aren't just horses and adjusts her schema, eventually being able to tell the difference between Bambi and Black Beauty. ✓

## 19. Monkeys and Morality
1. Summary of the Harlow Rhesus Monkey Experiment: The Harlows separated newborn Rhesus macaque monkeys from their mothers and gave them two artificial "mothers" — one was a wire cylinder with a feeding bottle (Wire Mother), and the other was a cloth-and-foam-wrapped cylinder without a bottle (Cloth Mother). The babies overwhelmingly preferred the Cloth Mother for comfort and security, even feeding from the Wire Mother while standing on the Cloth one. ✓
2. The finding of the experiment was that contact and touch are vital to attachment. ✓
3. A critical period is when certain things have to happen for normal development to occur. ✓
4. Just after hatching, when they accept the first moving object they see as their mother (imprinting). ✓
5. This is an example of imprinting, which is the process by which certain animals form an attachment to the first moving object they see during a critical period early in life. ✓
6. Secure (70%): Children happily explore and interact with strangers while mom is present; may get upset when mom leaves but greet her return happily and positively. Insecure/Ambivalent (15%): Afraid of strangers, cry more, explore less, have a major meltdown when mom leaves, then act angry or resistant when she returns. Insecure/Avoidant (15%): Indifferent to strangers, don't cling to mom, don't seem bothered when she leaves, and show little interest when she returns. ✓
7. Authoritarian: strict rules, "because I said so," not very warm. Permissive: caves to child's demands, little control. Authoritative: demanding but explains rules, loving and responsive. ✓
8. Kohlberg's Moral Development Theory: ✓
    - a. Preconventional (before age 9): kids are concerned with self-interest.
    - b. Conventional (early adolescence): emphasis on conformity (worrying about what others think).
    - c. Postconventional (adolescence and beyond): account for differing values, basic rights, and universal ethical principles.
