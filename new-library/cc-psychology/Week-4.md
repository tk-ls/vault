## 11. How to Train a Brain
1. Behaviourism is an empirically rigorous science focused on observable behaviours and NOT unobservable internal mental process. ✓
2. See here: ✓
	* unconditioned stimulus = food/meat powder smell
	* unconditioned (natural) response = drooling/salivation
	* neutral stimulus = bell sound/ringing
	* conditioned stimulus = bell sound (after conditioning)
	* conditioned response = drooling (to the bell)
3. Classical Conditioning is a type of learning in which one learns to associate events and anticipate events. ✓
4. Summarise John Watson's Little Albert Experiment: Watson conditioned a young child to fear a white rat by pairing the rat with a loud scary noise repeatedly. The fear then generalised to other furry white objects like bunnies, dogs, and fur coats. ✓
5. Operant Conditioning is a type of learning in which behaviour is strengthened if followed by reinforcement/reward or diminished if followed by punishment. ✓
6. Positive Reinforcement: a positive reinforcer is a stimulus that, when presented after a response, strengthens/increases the behaviour (by providing a reward). ✓
7. Shaping is an operant conditioning procedure in which reinforcers guide behaviour towards closer and closer approximations of the desired behaviour. ✓
8. Negative Reinforcement: a negative reinforcer is any stimulus that, when removed/taken away, strengthens the response. ✓
9. The car beeping until you fasten your seat belt - the behaviour (fastening seat belt) is reinforced by removing the annoying beeping sound. Also: painkillers that end a headache. ✓
10. Primary reinforcers satisfy innate biological needs and don't need to be learned (food, ending pain/annoyance). Conditioned/secondary reinforcers are learned - we associate them with primary reinforcers (like money/paychecks, which we want because we need food and shelter). ✓
11. Continuous reinforcement gives a reward every single time the behaviour occurs - learning is quick but extinction happens fast when rewards stop. Partial/intermittent reinforcement only occurs sometimes - takes longer to learn but holds up better in the long run and is more resistant to extinction. ✓
## 12. The Bobo Beat-down
1. Summarise the Albert Bandura Bobo Doll Experiment: Children watched an adult either beating/being aggressive toward an inflatable clown (Bobo), playing nicely with it, or ignoring it. After watching, children were frustrated by having fun toys taken away and left alone with Bobo. Kids who observed aggressive behaviour were much more likely to mimic that aggression (kicking, punching, using hostile language, even trying to use a hammer), while those who saw nice or neutral behaviour didn't respond aggressively. ✓
2. Species can more easily learn associations that help them thrive or survive, and that not all associations are learned equally. ✓
3. Hank says that, "sometimes we can think our way out of intended associations." What example does he give to demonstrate this? A person being treated for alcohol addiction might be given alcohol laced with a nauseating drug. While classical conditioning would suggest they'd associate alcohol with feeling sick, the brain can override this if it's aware the added drug (not the alcohol) is causing the illness. ✓
