## 13. How We Make Memories
1. Memories can be accessed through three primary methods: Recall – a measure of memory in which the person must **retrieve information without cues** Example: Fill-in-the-blank tests (Athens is the capital of Greece) ✓
2. Recognition – a measure of memory in which the person need only **identify information when presented with it** Example: Multiple-choice tests ✓
3. Relearning (or rehearsal) – a measure of memory that assesses the amount of time saved when **relearning previously learned information** Example: Studying for a final exam ✓
4. External events come into your Sensory Memory first, then go to your **short-term/working memory**, and then potentially into your **long-term memory**. ✓
5. Short-term memory is now better known as **working memory**. ✓
6. Explicit Memory is **information/memory** that one can consciously "know" and declare. Example - Facts like "Athens is the capital of Greece" ✓
7. Implicit Memory is retention **of information through automatic/non-conscious processing**, primarily through automatic processing. Example - Classically conditioned associations, procedural memories ✓
## 14. Remembering and Forgetting
1. Priming is activating associations **non-consciously (or unconsciously)**. ✓
2. One type of priming is context-dependent memory. What example does Hank Green give to demonstrate this? **Looking for a pen, getting distracted and ending up in the kitchen, then returning to bed and remembering why you got up** ✓
3. State-dependent and mood-congruent memories are other examples of priming that show our states and our emotions can also serve as **retrieval cues**. ✓
4. The serial position effect is our tendency to recall best the **first** and **last** items on a list. ✓
5. The first words are the **primacy effect** and the last words are the **recency effect**. ✓
6. What are the three ways we can experience memory failure? **Encoding failure, retrieval failure, and storage decay** ✓
7. Proactive interference, or forward acting interference, is the disruptive effect of prior learning on the recall of new information. What example does Hank Green give for this concept? **Recalling an old password when trying to remember a new one** ✓
8. Hank talks about both the misinformation effect and source misattribution (aka source amnesia). These are both summarised by completing the following statement Hank reveals toward the end of the video: Memory is both a **reconstruction** and a **reproduction**. ✓
## 15. How Your Mind Can Amaze and Betray You
1. Cognition is defined as our **knowing**, **remembering**, and **understanding/communicating**. ✓
2. A concept is a **mental grouping** of similar objects, people, ideas, or events. A prototype is the mental image or **best/pinnacle example** of the concept. ✓
3. How do algorithms differ from heuristics? **Algorithms are logical, methodical, step-by-step procedures that guarantee a solution but are slower; heuristics are mental shortcuts that are faster but more error-prone** ✓
4. Belief Perseverance is the tendency to cling to initial conceptions or beliefs despite proof to the contrary. What example does Hank Green use to demonstrate this? **People who still believe the Earth is flat despite space pictures/evidence** ✓
5. The availability heuristic is a mental shortcut relying on psychologically salient or easily imagined examples rather than actual odds or factual information. How does Hank Green exemplify this concept? **Casinos making wins vivid with lights and bells while losses are silent, making people overestimate their chances of winning** ✓
6. Framing is the idea that how an issue is posed or presented can significantly affect decisions and judgments. What is an example of time you've used framing to your advantage in some way? **Framing risks as "95% survival rate" vs "5 out of 100 die".** ✓
## 16. Language
1. Phonemes are short, distinctive, **sound units**. Four examples given: a, t, ch, sh ✓
2. Roughly how many phonemes are in the English language? **40** ✓
3. Morphemes are the smallest units that **carry meaning** (or **have meaning**). These can be words, or parts of words, such as a **prefix** or a **suffix**. ✓
4. The morpheme speech has what phonemes? **s, p, ee, ch (4 phonemes)** ✓
5. Morphemes are arranged into a language's grammar: a **system of rules** that enables us to communicate with and understand others. ✓
6. Around 4 months, infants develop **receptive language**: the ability to understand what's being said both to, and about, us. ✓
7. The first stage of language development at 4 months old is the **babbling** stage, but it is NOT an imitation of adult speech. ✓
8. What happens if the child isn't exposed to other languages? **They lose the ability to hear and create particular tones and sounds that aren't part of their household language** ✓
9. Between ages 1 and 2, children enter the **one-word** stage. ✓
10. The **two-word (or telegraphic speech)** stage begins after about age 2. ✓
11. What is telegraphic speech? **Choppy sentences using mostly nouns and verbs, like "Want juice" or "No pants"** ✓
12. A neurological impairment of language is known as **aphasia**. ✓
13. Damage to Broca's area impairs **speech production** (or **speaking**). ✓
14. Damage to Wernicke's area impairs **language comprehension** (or **understanding language/making sense**). ✓
