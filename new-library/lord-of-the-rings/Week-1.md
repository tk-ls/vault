---

1. To most hobbits, Frodo being Bilbo’s favourite and heir made him a subject of curiosity and envy. For the Sackville-Bagginses (Otho and Lobelia), the relationship was a source of bitter resentment. They considered themselves the rightful heirs to Bag End and were furious when Bilbo adopted Frodo, as it legally blocked them from moving into the "luxurious" hobbit-hole they had coveted for decades. ==The Sackville-Bagginses are jealous of Frodo because Bilbo chooses him as his heir, which dashes their hopes of inheriting Bag End.==
2. Gandalf generally supports Bilbo’s desire for a "holiday," but he is stern and insistent regarding the Ring. He recognises that the Ring has gained too much power over Bilbo. While he wants Bilbo to find peace, he acts as a firm moral anchor, pushing Bilbo to leave the Ring behind for Frodo—a moment that tests their friendship but ultimately ensures Bilbo's freedom from its corruption. ==Gandalf is supportive but also concerned about Bilbo’s use of the ring and its hold on him. He encourages Bilbo to leave the ring behind.==
3. The hobbits notice that as Frodo approaches fifty (the hobbit middle-age), he looks exactly the same as he did when he was twenty-one. They attribute this to his wealth and "easy living," but the real reason is the One Ring. Like Bilbo before him, the Ring prevents Frodo from aging naturally, "preserving" him in a state of unnatural youth. ==The hobbits suspect Frodo’s preservation is due to some kind of magic or luck, much like they thought of Bilbo.==
4. During their long talk in Bag End, Gandalf explains that the Ring is not just a trinket; it is the Master Ring forged by the Dark Lord Sauron. He explains:
	- It holds a great part of Sauron's own power.
	- It is sentient and seeks to return to its master.
	- It corrupts anyone who uses it, eventually "fading" them into a wraith.
	- The Enemy is now actively searching for it because it is the key to his total dominion over Middle-earth.
5. Frodo experiences a pull in two directions. On one hand, he loves the Shire—its peace, its woods, and his friends. On the other hand, he inherited Bilbo's "restlessness." As the shadow of Mordor grows, he feels a sense of burden and duty; he knows that staying puts the Shire in danger, but leaving means walking into a terrifying, unknown world. ==Frodo feels reluctant to leave the Shire because it has been his home for so long, and he loves the peaceful life there, but he also understands the danger the Ring poses. 
6. Gildor Inglorion, the Elf, recognises that Frodo’s plight is much more serious than a simple "disappearance." He notes that the Black Riders are the "Nine Servants of the Lord of the Rings." By choosing to call Frodo "Elf-friend," he signals that Frodo is now part of a much larger, ancient struggle against the Enemy, and he warns Frodo that the Shire is no longer a safe fortress against the outside world. ==Gildor explains that the Enemy is pursuing Frodo and warns him that peril lies ahead, urging him to be cautious and to flee the Black Riders.==
7. Frodo’s decision is driven by fear and caution. After seeing the Black Rider on the road, he feels exposed and "hunted." He believes that by leaving the main Road and cutting across the country, he can shake off any pursuit. He is willing to risk the physical difficulty of the terrain to avoid being seen by the terrifying riders. ==Frodo prefers the shortcut because he believes they will be less likely to encounter the Black Riders if they stay off the main roads.==
8. Frodo’s fear of Maggot and his dogs (Grip, Fang, and Wolf) highlights his mischievous but vulnerable childhood. As an orphan living in Buckland, Frodo was a bit of a "troublemaker" who used to steal Maggot's mushrooms. It shows that despite his high status as a Baggins, Frodo was a spirited and slightly rebellious youth who still carries the relatable, small-scale fears of his upbringing. ==Frodo’s fear of Farmer Maggot’s dogs highlights his mischievous past in Buckland, where he trespassed to steal mushrooms, leading to his fear of being caught again.==
